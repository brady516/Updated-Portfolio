# Voice & brand — North de Noise

**Publication:** North de Noise.
**House voice / ethos:** *Middle Finger Finance.*
**Tagline of the voice:** **Strategic Irreverence Grounded in Virtue.**

North de Noise is the name on the door. Middle Finger Finance is how it talks.

## The whole idea, in one breath

Irreverence earns the attention. Virtue earns the trust. Either one alone is a
failure mode: virtue without irreverence is the beige research nobody reads;
irreverence without virtue is just another loudmouth with a ring light. We do both
on purpose. We get to flip off the priesthood *because* we show the receipts.

## The voice

Dry. Deadpan. Satirical. Irreverent. Witty. **Economical.** Closer to a
short-tempered desk analyst who's read the filing than to a hype account that hasn't.
The funniest sentence and the most rigorous sentence are often the same sentence.

If you're choosing between a clever line and a clear one, pick the line that's both.
If you can't, pick clear — clarity is the virtue; cleverness is the seasoning.

## The four rules (non-negotiable)

1. **Punch up, never down.** Target the jargon, the guru-industrial complex, the
   "this stock will 10x" merchants, the earnings-call word salad. **Never the
   reader.** The reader is on our side of the joke, not the receiving end of it.

2. **Virtue is not a punchline.** Disclaimers, risk, "no guarantees," conflict
   disclosures, "past performance" — always played straight. The contrast is the
   brand: savage about the nonsense, surgical about the truth. Never wink your way
   out of a risk disclosure.

3. **Earn the snark with substance.** Every irreverent line is backed by a real
   number, a real mechanism, or a real point. Wit without rigor is the exact noise
   we mock. No empty dunks.

4. **Dry beats loud.** Understatement over exclamation points. One clean stiletto
   over ten foam bats. Cut adjectives. If a sentence isn't carrying information or a
   laugh, it's carrying nothing — delete it. No emoji confetti.

## Devices that fit

- **Deadpan translation:** quote the jargon, then say what it actually means in
  flat plain English. (This is literally the Word Salad segment.)
- **The setup-then-stiletto:** a straight sentence, then one short line that pops it.
- **Calling the thing what it is:** "a price target is a guess wearing a tie."
- **Earned self-deprecation:** occasionally turn the knife on ourselves — it proves
  we're not selling a guru. Keeps the virtue honest.

## Don'ts

- Don't punch at retail investors, beginners, or anyone earnestly trying.
- Don't manufacture outrage or hype — that's the competition's product.
- Don't let a joke soften, hedge, or obscure a risk disclosure or a number.
- Don't overstay a bit. One good line, move on.
- Don't be edgy for edge's sake. Irreverence is *strategic* — it serves the point.

## Before / after

> **Beige:** "It is important to remember that past performance does not guarantee
> future results, and investors should carefully consider their risk tolerance."
>
> **In voice:** "Past performance guarantees nothing — it's the one sentence in
> finance everybody quotes and nobody believes. Believe it." *(Then the actual
> disclaimer, played straight.)*

> **Beige:** "This valuation suggests the stock may be overvalued at current levels."
>
> **In voice:** "Run the reverse-DCF and the 'cheap' stock is quietly pricing in a
> decade of flawless execution and a small miracle. Cheap relative to what, exactly?"

> **Beige:** "Many online finance creators lack proper credentials."
>
> **In voice:** "The median finance guru has a thumbnail, a Lamborghini rental, and
> no CRD number. I'll show you mine."

## The litmus test

Read it back. If it made you smirk *and* you learned something true *and* it
wouldn't embarrass us in front of a compliance officer — ship it. If it only does
one of the three, it's not done.
