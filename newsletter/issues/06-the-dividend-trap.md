# The dividend trap

A high dividend yield is finance's most flattering lie. It looks like income; it's
often a countdown. The yield is just dividend ÷ price — and when a price falls
because the business is in trouble, the yield *rises*, right up until the dividend
gets cut and takes the price down with it. The screen says "8%." The screen is not
your friend.

## Signal vs. Noise — "8% yield, safe forever" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** rank by yield, buy the top of the list, call it a portfolio.

The highest yields on any screen are highest for a reason, and the reason is rarely
"generosity." A fat yield is the market pricing in a cut you haven't admitted yet.

> **The Signal:** a dividend is only as safe as the cash flow behind it. Yield is
> the headline; **payout ratio and free cash flow** are the story.

## The Number — the payout ratio *(free)*

Payout ratio = dividends ÷ earnings (better: ÷ free cash flow). Comfortably under
**~60–70%** leaves room to keep paying through a rough patch. Over **100%**, the
company is paying you with money it doesn't have — which is a loan from the future,
not a dividend.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"The board remains committed to returning capital to
shareholders and to a sustainable, well-covered dividend."*

**Translation:** "We really hope we don't have to cut this."

**What it's hiding:** "committed to" is not "able to," and the word "sustainable"
appears most often in the press release right before it isn't. Check coverage
yourself — the board's feelings don't pay your dividend.

## The Read — telling income from a trap *(paid)*

Before you trust a yield, run it past four checks:

- **Free-cash-flow coverage.** Does operating cash flow comfortably cover the
  dividend *and* necessary capex? Earnings can be massaged; cash less so.
- **Payout-ratio trend.** Creeping toward 100% over a few years is a slow-motion
  warning, not a yield.
- **Debt and maturities.** A company servicing a wall of debt will protect bondholders
  before shareholders. The dividend is the first thing overboard.
- **Dividend history through a recession.** Did they hold (or grow) the payout in
  2008/2020, or cut it? Behavior under stress is the only track record that counts.

## What I'd Watch

Take the highest yielder you own or covet. Find its free-cash-flow payout ratio. If
it's over 100%, you're not collecting income — you're first in line for a haircut.
