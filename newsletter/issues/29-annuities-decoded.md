# Annuities decoded: when the guarantee is worth it

Annuities are either a rip-off or a lifesaver, depending entirely on which annuity and
who's selling it. The word covers everything from a clean, cheap "paycheck for life"
to a Frankenstein of riders and surrender charges built mostly to pay a commission.
With my insurance license, I'll tell you the part the brochure skips: most of the
confusion is on purpose.

## Signal vs. Noise — "guaranteed income for life!" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "lifetime income, guaranteed, market upside with no downside!"

"No downside with upside" is the phrase that should make you check your wallet. Every
guarantee is *paid for* — in caps on your gains, in fees, in surrender lock-ups. The
insurer is not a charity; the guarantee has a price, and it's usually you who pays it.

> **The Signal:** an annuity is *insurance against outliving your money,* not an
> investment that beats the market. Judged as insurance, a few are great. Judged as an
> investment, most are bad.

## The Number — the rider stack *(free)*

A variable or indexed annuity can carry **2–4%/yr in combined fees** (M&E, admin,
rider, fund costs) — a brutal drag (Issue #14) on something also sold for "growth." A
plain immediate annuity, by contrast, has almost no ongoing fee — it just converts a
lump sum into a lifelong check. Same word, wildly different products.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week — Annuity Edition *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"A principal-protected accumulation vehicle with market-linked
upside and a guaranteed lifetime withdrawal benefit."*

**Translation:** "An expensive product that caps your gains and charges you for a
promise."

**What it's hiding:** "market-linked upside" almost always means *capped* upside
(you get some of the gain, none of the dividends, with a ceiling). "Guaranteed
withdrawal benefit" is a rider you pay for annually. Strip the nouns and ask: *what's
the cap, what's the fee, and what's the surrender period?*

## The Read — the good, the bad, the test *(paid)*

**The genuinely useful one — a SPIA (single-premium immediate annuity):** hand over a
lump sum, get a fixed check for life. Cheap, simple, and it does the one thing a
portfolio can't *promise* — pay you no matter how long you live. Great for covering
*essential* expenses as a "floor" (pairs with Issue #28: insure the necessities,
invest the rest).

**The usually-bad ones — variable and indexed annuities** loaded with riders, caps,
and surrender charges. High fees, opaque terms, and a commission that explains the
enthusiasm. Occasionally fit a niche; mostly they're sold, not bought.

**The test:**
- **Is it solving "I might outlive my money"?** Then a *simple* annuity (SPIA) may
  genuinely help.
- **Is it pitched as "growth" or "market upside"?** Now it's an investment in
  disguise — compare it honestly to a low-cost portfolio and watch the fees decide it.
- **Surrender period and commission?** A long surrender schedule (your money locked
  for years with a penalty) usually signals a big upfront commission you're funding.
- **Could a fee-only advisor or a plain SPIA do the same job cheaper?** Usually yes.

Annuities aren't evil — *outliving your money* is a real risk, and insuring it is
legitimate. The trick is buying the simple insurance you need, not the complex
investment someone needs to sell.

## What I'd Watch

If you're shown an annuity, ask for three numbers in writing: **total annual fees, the
cap/participation rate on "upside," and the surrender schedule + commission.** If
getting those feels like pulling teeth, the product is complex *for a reason* — and the
reason isn't you.
