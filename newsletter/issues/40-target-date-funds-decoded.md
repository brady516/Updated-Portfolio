# Target-date funds: the good default, decoded

The target-date fund is the rare Wall Street product that's actually *good* for most
people — a one-ticket, auto-adjusting, diversified portfolio that does the boring right
things on autopilot. Which is exactly why it's worth understanding: a good default
still has a fee, an assumption set, and a couple of traps, and "good for most" isn't
"optimal for you."

## Signal vs. Noise — "set it and forget it, you're done" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "Pick the fund with your retirement year, never think again."

For a hands-off investor, that's *genuinely close to correct* — and far better than the
tinkering most people would otherwise do (Issue #35). But "never think again" skips
checking the fee, the glide path, and whether holding it in a *taxable* account quietly
hurts you. A good default rewards five minutes of understanding.

> **The Signal:** a target-date fund is a *diversified portfolio that de-risks as you
> age,* in one ticker. Know its fee and its glide path, then mostly leave it alone.

## The Number — the fee gap *(free)*

Target-date fees range from **~0.08%** (index-based, e.g., the cheap providers) to
**~0.60%+** (active versions). Same idea, ~7x the cost — and that gap compounds into a
chunk of your retirement (Issue #14). The product being "good" doesn't mean every
*version* is; the cheap index ones are the win, the pricey active ones less so.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our actively managed target-date series leverages tactical
asset allocation and proprietary glide-path research to optimize outcomes across the
participant lifecycle."*

**Translation:** "Our target-date fund costs more because we trade inside it."

**What it's hiding:** "tactical asset allocation" inside a target-date fund means
*active bets and higher fees* wrapped in a product people buy precisely to *avoid*
active bets. "Proprietary glide-path research" is mostly marketing — the glide paths
across providers are broadly similar; the fee difference is not.

## The Read — using the good default well *(paid)*

- **Buy the index version, mind the fee.** An index-based target-date fund at ~0.10%
  is one of the best one-decision products in finance. Active ones at 0.50%+ usually
  aren't worth the premium.
- **Know your fund's glide path.** "To" vs. "through" retirement: some funds reach
  their most conservative mix *at* the target year; others keep de-risking for years
  *past* it. That changes your equity exposure right in the sequence-risk window (Issue
  #24) — make sure it matches your plan.
- **"Pick your year" is a starting point, not a law.** The year on the label assumes a
  generic risk tolerance. If you're more aggressive (or conservative) than average,
  it's fine to pick a fund dated later (or earlier) to shift the stock/bond mix.
- **Don't mix it with a pile of other funds.** The whole point is that it's the *entire*
  portfolio. Bolting six other funds onto a target-date fund undoes its allocation and
  recreates the overlap mess (Issue #14). Use it *or* build your own — not a Frankenstein
  of both.
- **Watch the account.** Target-date funds can be tax-inefficient (they rebalance and
  hold bonds), so they shine in 401(k)/IRA accounts and can drag in *taxable* ones
  (Issue #18). Asset location still applies.

It's the right answer for most people *because* it forces the boring discipline —
diversification, automatic de-risking, no tinkering. Just buy the cheap version, match
the glide path to your plan, and let it be the one decision it was designed to be.

## What I'd Watch

If you own a target-date fund, check two things: its **expense ratio** (is it the
~0.10% index version or a pricey active one?) and its **glide path** ("to" or
"through"?). Five minutes tells you whether you own the great default or an expensive
impersonation of it.
