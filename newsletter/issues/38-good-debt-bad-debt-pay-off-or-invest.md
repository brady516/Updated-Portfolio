# Good debt, bad debt, and "should I pay off the mortgage?"

Debt is neither virtue nor sin — it's a tool with an interest rate. The useful
question is never "is debt bad?" (a slogan) but "does this debt's rate beat what I'd do
with the money instead, after tax and after how it makes me feel?" Get that framing
right and the endless "pay off vs. invest" debate mostly answers itself.

## Signal vs. Noise — "all debt is bad / debt is free money" *(free)*

> **The Noise (one camp):** "Pay off every debt immediately, debt is bondage."
> **The Noise (other camp):** "Never pay off cheap debt, leverage everything."

Both skip the only variable that matters: **the rate, after tax, vs. your alternative
use of the dollar.** A 24% credit card and a 3% mortgage are not the same animal, and
treating them with one rule is how people either stay broke or over-leverage.

> **The Signal:** rank debts by *after-tax interest rate.* Kill the high stuff fast;
> the cheap stuff is a math problem, not a morality play.

## The Number — the guaranteed return *(free)*

Paying off a debt is a **guaranteed, risk-free, after-tax return equal to its
interest rate.** Crushing a 22% credit card = a guaranteed 22% return — better than any
investment you'll reliably find. A 3% mortgage = a guaranteed 3%, which a diversified
portfolio has historically beaten over long horizons. The rate *is* the hurdle.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Leverage your home equity to put your money to work and
optimize your balance sheet's efficiency."*

**Translation:** "Borrow against your house to invest, and pay us either way."

**What it's hiding:** "optimize balance-sheet efficiency" makes *taking on risk* sound
like tidying a spreadsheet. Borrowing to invest can work mathematically and *also* turn
a market dip into a margin-style nightmare against your *home* (Issues #16, #47). The
phrase sells the upside of leverage and mutes the part where it cuts both ways.

## The Read — the actual decision framework *(paid)*

Roughly in priority order:
1. **Employer match first.** Free 50–100% return; nothing beats it. Capture it before
   anything else.
2. **Kill high-interest debt (≳7–8%).** Credit cards, personal loans — the guaranteed
   return crushes anything you'd earn investing. This is the clearest win in personal
   finance.
3. **Build the emergency fund** (Issue #30) so a surprise doesn't put you *back* on the
   high-interest cards.
4. **Then it's genuinely a toss-up — and personal.** For low-rate debt (a sub-~5%
   mortgage), the *math* often favors investing the difference (expected return >
   rate). The *emotion* often favors paying it down (a paid-off house helps people
   sleep, and "sleep" has real value). Both are defensible — there's no shame in buying
   peace of mind with a few basis points of expected return.

The honest nuances most takes skip:
- **After-tax matters.** A deductible mortgage's *effective* rate may be lower than the
  sticker; a card's is brutally real. Compare apples to apples.
- **Guaranteed vs. expected.** Paying debt is a *sure* return; investing is an
  *expected* one with risk. Risk-adjust before you call investing the "winner."
- **Behavior is a real input, not a weakness.** If carrying debt makes you anxious
  enough to invest badly, paying it down *is* the optimal move — for you.

## What I'd Watch

List every debt with its rate, highest first. Anything above ~7–8% is an emergency to
extinguish; the cheap stuff is a calm pay-down-or-invest choice you can make on math
*and* temperament. If you don't know your rates off the top of your head, that's the
first thing to fix — you can't rank what you haven't measured.
