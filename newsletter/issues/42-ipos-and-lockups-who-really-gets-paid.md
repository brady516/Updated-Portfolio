# IPOs and lockups: who really gets paid

An IPO is sold as your chance to "get in early." Be honest about the seating chart:
by the time a company IPOs, the *actually* early money — founders, employees, venture
capital — has been in for years and is, on average, *selling* to you. The IPO isn't the
start of the party. For the insiders, it's often the exit.

## Signal vs. Noise — "get in on the IPO before it pops" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "IPOs pop on day one — get an allocation and ride it."

The day-one "pop" mostly benefits the banks' favored institutional clients who got
shares at the offer price. Retail typically buys *after* the pop, at the open — i.e.,
at the high. And "get in early" is backwards: the IPO is insiders *cashing out* to new
buyers, structured and timed by people whose job is to sell it at the best price *for
the seller.*

> **The Signal:** at an IPO you're usually the *buyer of last resort* in a sale designed
> by the seller. Ask *who's selling, how much, and what do they know that you don't?*

## The Number — the lockup cliff *(free)*

Insiders are usually barred from selling for a **lockup period (~90–180 days)** after
the IPO. Watch that expiration date: when the lockup lifts, a wave of insider shares can
hit the market, and prices often **sag around the expiry** as early holders finally
sell. The calendar tells you when the *real* float arrives — and it's public.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week — IPO Edition *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"This generational company is going public to accelerate its
mission and give the broader market access to its hypergrowth story."*

**Translation:** "Early investors want to sell, and the market's hot."

**What it's hiding:** "give the broader market access" reframes *insiders cashing out*
as generosity. Companies tend to IPO when *valuations are friendly to sellers* — which
is precisely when buyers should be most careful. "Hypergrowth story" is the narrative;
the prospectus (S-1) has the numbers the story is steering you past.

## The Read — reading an IPO honestly *(paid)*

- **Read the S-1 like a skeptic.** Especially: are they *profitable* or burning cash?
  How fast is the burn? What's the *path* to profit, not just the growth rate (Issue
  #2's required-assumptions table applies)?
- **Follow the share count and the sellers.** Who's selling in the offering vs. raising
  primary capital for the business? Heavy *secondary* selling (insiders cashing out) is
  a different signal than money going *into* the company.
- **Mind the dual-class structure.** Many IPOs keep super-voting shares for founders —
  you may be buying economics with little control. Fine if you know it; a surprise if you
  don't.
- **Respect the lockup calendar.** Know when insider shares unlock; expect supply (and
  often weakness) around it. Don't confuse a thin early float's price with real demand.
- **You can almost always wait.** There's no prize for buying on day one. Let the lockup
  pass, let a few earnings reports arrive as a *public* company, and buy with real
  numbers and a real float. The great companies are still buyable later; you just miss
  the hype tax.

None of this means every IPO is a trap — some are genuinely great businesses. It means
the *structure* is built for the seller, the hype is loudest exactly when caution is
most warranted, and "early" is a story you're being told, not a seat you actually have.

## What I'd Watch

Next hot IPO, ask three things: *Is the company selling shares, or are insiders? Is it
profitable or burning cash? When does the lockup expire?* If the answers are "insiders /
burning / soon," you're not getting in early — you're the liquidity the early money was
waiting for.
