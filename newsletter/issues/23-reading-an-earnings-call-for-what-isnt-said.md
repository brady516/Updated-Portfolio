# Reading an earnings call for what isn't said

The 10-K is what a company is forced to write down. The earnings call is what
management chooses to *say out loud* — and, more revealingly, what they carefully
don't. The prepared remarks are theater. The real information is in the Q&A, in the
dodge, in the question that gets thanked-for-and-not-answered.

## Signal vs. Noise — "we're pleased with our progress" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** the scripted optimism. "We're very pleased with our momentum and
> excited about the opportunities ahead."

That sentence is identical across ten thousand calls and contains zero information by
design. Prepared remarks are a press release read aloud. Nodding along to them is how
you learn nothing, confidently.

> **The Signal:** the **Q&A** is the call. Analysts ask the uncomfortable questions;
> *how* management answers — directly, or with a fog machine — is the data.

## The Number — the non-answer *(free)*

Count how many analyst questions get a *specific number* versus a *narrative.* When a
direct question ("what were unit economics in the new segment?") gets a paragraph
about "strategic momentum" and no figure, that's not an oversight. Specifics they're
proud of get quoted; specifics they're not get fogged. The fog is the answer.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Yeah, thanks for the question. We're really pleased with the
trajectory, and while we're not breaking that out today, we feel great about the
long-term framework and the optionality it creates."*

**Translation:** "The number is bad and I'm not telling you."

**What it's hiding:** "not breaking that out today" is the single most useful phrase
on any call — it's a company declining to share a metric it *has.* Companies trumpet
good numbers and bury bad ones in "framework" and "trajectory." The refusal *is* the
disclosure.

## The Read — how to actually listen *(paid)*

- **Skip to the Q&A.** Read prepared remarks at 2x or skim the transcript; spend your
  attention where the analysts push.
- **Watch for evasion patterns:** "not breaking that out," "directionally," "puts and
  takes," "we'll circle back offline," repeating the question as a stall, answering a
  *different, easier* question than the one asked.
- **Track what changed in the language.** Compare this call to last quarter's. A metric
  they used to lead with that's quietly gone missing is a flashing light. New hedges
  around a previously confident topic, ditto.
- **Note who's dodging.** A CFO who suddenly hands every hard number to the CEO's
  "color" is a tell. Confidence delegates specifics; nervousness delegates them away.
- **Hear the tone on guidance.** "We're comfortable with the range" vs. "we're
  reiterating guidance" vs. silence on guidance — each is a different temperature.

None of this requires a Bloomberg terminal. It requires listening for the gap between
the question asked and the question answered. That gap is where the truth lives.

## What I'd Watch

Pull the last earnings-call transcript of a company you own and read **only the Q&A.**
Mark every question that got a story instead of a number. If the fog is thick, the
stock's narrative and its reality are drifting apart — and the call just told you,
without saying it.
