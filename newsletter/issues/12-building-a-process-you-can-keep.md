# Building a process you can keep

*Monthly flagship — the long one. And the one that ties the first quarter together.*

We've spent twelve weeks taking the noise apart: 10x pitches, tortured DCFs, yield
traps, flattering backtests, your own behavioral wiring. Here's the synthesis. None
of it matters without the one thing almost nobody builds — a **written process you'll
actually follow when the money gets emotional.** Rules made in peacetime are the only
ones that survive the war.

## Signal vs. Noise — "just hold and don't look" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "just hold and don't look" as the entire plan.

It's well-meaning, and for many people indexing-and-ignoring genuinely beats
tinkering. But "don't look" is not a process — it's an instruction to dissociate, and
it shatters the first time the market gives you a real reason to look. A plan you
abandon under stress was never a plan.

> **The Signal:** a written process tells you *what to do when you do look* — so the
> looking doesn't turn into panicking.

## The Number — one page *(free)*

Your entire investment policy should fit on **one page.** If it needs ten, you won't
follow it. Goals, allocation, rules for buying, rules for selling, rebalancing
cadence. One page, written when calm, re-read when not.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our disciplined, repeatable investment process leverages a
robust framework to navigate all market environments."*

**Translation:** "We have a process. We promise. It's very process-y."

**What it's hiding:** every firm claims a "disciplined, repeatable process"; almost
none will show you the one page. A real process is *specific and falsifiable* — "we
rebalance when an asset drifts 5% from target" — not a string of reassuring
adjectives. If they can't state a rule you could check, there isn't one.

## The Read — your one-page IPS *(paid)*

A personal Investment Policy Statement, in five lines:

1. **Goal & horizon.** What the money is for and when you need it. Everything else
   serves this.
2. **Target allocation.** The mix you'll hold, with ranges. This is where most of
   your return and risk actually comes from — not stock picking.
3. **Buy rules.** What has to be true to add (a thesis, a valuation, a contribution
   schedule). "I felt like it" is not a rule.
4. **Sell rules.** What breaks a thesis; what triggers a trim. Decided *before* you
   own it, so the exit isn't an emotion.
5. **Rebalancing cadence.** When and how you return to target — on a date, or on a
   drift threshold. This forces buy-low/sell-high mechanically, which is the opposite
   of what your instincts will beg for.

That's it. The genius isn't in the document; it's in having decided *in advance*, so
the market's worst day finds you already knowing what to do. Everything in this first
quarter — valuation, risk, behavior — was just teaching you what to *put* on the page.

## What I'd Watch

Write your one page this week. Not a perfect one — a real one. The investor with a
mediocre plan they follow beats the genius who improvises in a drawdown, every time.
That's the whole game, and now you've got the playbook for it.
