# Monte Carlo vs. the one rosy line

Open almost any retirement projection and you'll see it: a single smooth line
arcing confidently up and to the right, ending at a comfortable number in 2055. It's
reassuring. It's also a lie of omission — because the future doesn't come in one
line. It comes in thousands of possible lines, and the spread between them is the
whole story your planner's chart politely deleted.

## Signal vs. Noise — "you'll have $2.1M at 65" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** the point estimate. "Assuming 7% a year, you'll have $2.1M."

Markets don't return 7% a year. They return 22%, then -18%, then 9%, then -4% — and
*the order matters enormously.* A single average baked into a straight line hides the
one thing that decides whether you're fine or eating cat food: the range of outcomes,
and where you land in it.

> **The Signal:** a probability, not a promise. "In ~80% of simulated futures you're
> fine; here's what the other 20% looks like and what to do about it."

## The Number — the 7% that never happens *(free)*

The market's long-run *average* might be ~7% real-ish, but the number of individual
years that actually landed near 7%? Vanishingly few. The average is made of extremes.
Planning on the average is like planning to cross a river that's "four feet deep on
average" — true, and irrelevant to whether you drown.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Based on our proprietary planning model, you are on track to
meet your retirement goals with a high degree of confidence."*

**Translation:** "We ran one optimistic line and it ended above your number."

**What it's hiding:** "high degree of confidence" is doing enormous unearned work. A
projection without a *probability* and a *range* isn't confidence — it's a single
guess in a suit. Ask: "what percent of scenarios succeed, and what happens in the
bottom 10%?" Watch the confidence get specific or evaporate.

## The Read — running your future thousands of times *(paid)*

Monte Carlo simulation does the obvious-once-you-see-it thing: instead of one average
return, it runs your plan across *thousands* of randomized return sequences and asks
how often you don't run out of money. What you get back is honest:

- **A success probability** ("85% of paths leave you solvent at 95"), not a single
  ending balance.
- **The shape of failure** — *when* and *how* the bad 15% break, which tells you what
  to actually fix (save more, spend less, work two more years, de-risk near
  retirement).
- **Sensitivity** — change the savings rate or retirement age and watch the
  probability move. Now you're steering, not hoping.

The [Monte Carlo model in the store](../../store.html) does exactly this — stress-tests
a plan across regimes so you see the probability of success, not the single rosy line.
It won't tell you the future. It'll tell you the *odds*, which is the only honest thing
anyone can offer.

## What I'd Watch

Next time a projection shows you one confident line, ask for the **distribution**: what
fraction of outcomes succeed, and what does the bottom decile look like? If the answer
is a shrug, you weren't given a plan. You were given a brochure.
