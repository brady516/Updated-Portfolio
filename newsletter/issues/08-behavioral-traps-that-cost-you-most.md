# The behavioral traps that cost you most

*Monthly flagship — the long one.*

Here's the uncomfortable research: the average investor underperforms the average
*fund they own*, because they buy after it's gone up and sell after it's gone down.
The gap isn't a knowledge problem — plenty of smart people do it. It's a wiring
problem. You can't delete the wiring, but you can build a process that routes around
it. This issue is the field guide to your own worst instincts.

## Signal vs. Noise — "buy the dip" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "buy the dip" as a reflex, shouted at every red candle.

Sometimes the dip is a sale. Sometimes the dip is the beginning of the information.
"Buy the dip" with no thesis is just "buy," with extra confidence at the worst
possible moment. The slogan does the thinking so you don't have to — which is the
problem.

> **The Signal:** a dip is only a buy if your *reason* for owning it is still true.
> Reassess the thesis, not the candle.

## The Number — the behavior gap *(free)*

Study after study pegs the investor-vs-investment return gap at roughly **1–3% a
year**, lost almost entirely to badly-timed buying and selling. Compounded over
decades, that gap is a house. You're not fighting the market for it; you're fighting
yourself.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We're being tactically opportunistic and waiting for more
constructive entry points amid the current risk-off backdrop."*

**Translation:** "We got scared and sold, and we're calling it strategy."

**What it's hiding:** "waiting for a better entry" is, nine times out of ten, the
fear of buying when it's uncomfortable — which is precisely when the returns hide.
"Tactically opportunistic" is the costume panic wears to the office.

## The Read — the four traps and the fixes *(paid)*

1. **Recency bias.** You extrapolate the recent past forever — bull markets feel
   permanent at the top, bear markets at the bottom. *Fix:* a written plan made when
   you're calm, followed when you're not.
2. **Loss aversion.** Losses hurt about twice as much as gains feel good, so you sell
   winners early and marry losers. *Fix:* pre-commit your exits by thesis, not by P&L
   color.
3. **Anchoring.** You fixate on the price you paid as if the market cares. *Fix:* ask
   "would I buy it here today?" Your cost basis is not a fact about the company.
4. **Confirmation bias.** You collect evidence you're right and mute the rest. *Fix:*
   write the bear case *yourself*, in advance — the strongest one you can.

The meta-fix under all four: **a process you decided on before the money got
emotional.** Rules made in peacetime are the only ones that survive the war. (That's
also next month's flagship.)

## What I'd Watch

Think back to your worst investing decision. Odds are it wasn't a bad *analysis* —
it was a bad *moment*, made emotionally. That's the enemy. It's beatable, but only
with a written process, not with more willpower.
