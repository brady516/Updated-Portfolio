# Stock-picking vs. indexing: the honest scorecard

*Monthly flagship — the long one, and a fight I'll referee fairly.*

This is the holy war of personal finance, and both sides lie a little. The indexers
pretend skill doesn't exist; the stock-pickers pretend they have it. The honest
scorecard is less satisfying than either camp's bumper sticker and far more useful.

## Signal vs. Noise — "I beat the market" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "I'm up 20% this year, I'm beating the market." Stated proudly,
> usually in a green year.

Beating the market means beating it *risk-adjusted, after fees and taxes, over a full
cycle including the bad years* — not posting a big number in a bull run by taking more
risk. Most "I beat the market" is a leverage or beta story wearing a skill costume.

> **The Signal:** measure against the right benchmark, net of everything, over years.
> Most who do that honestly discover they tied the index — and paid more to do it.

## The Number — the base rate *(free)*

Over long horizons, the large majority of professional active funds underperform their
benchmark after fees — and the few that win in one period rarely repeat. That's not an
insult; it's arithmetic. The market return *is* the average of all participants, so
after costs, the average participant must trail it. The house edge is the fee.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our active managers add value through superior security
selection and benchmark-aware risk management across the cycle."*

**Translation:** "We pick stocks, and we'll measure ourselves against the index we're
quietly hugging."

**What it's hiding:** "benchmark-aware" is the confession — a fund hugging the index
charges active fees for near-index returns (closet indexing again). "Adds value" is
asserted in the present tense for something only provable in the past, net of fees, and
usually isn't.

## The Read — the honest scorecard *(paid)*

**Default to the index for most of your money.** Low fee, low tax, no manager risk, you
capture the market and keep almost all of it. For the bulk of most people's wealth,
this is simply correct, and pretending otherwise is ego.

**But skill isn't zero — it's rare, and it's specific.** A genuine edge exists where:
- You have a real **informational or analytical advantage** (you understand an industry
  cold, you'll do work others won't).
- You have **structural advantages institutions lack** — patience, no career risk, no
  redemptions, the freedom to be weird and wait.
- You can **honestly measure** yourself and fire yourself when the scorecard says so.

So the mature answer isn't "index vs. pick." It's a **core and a satellite:** index the
core you can't afford to get wrong; reserve a smaller, sized "active" sleeve for the
genuine convictions where you have an edge — and *track that sleeve honestly* against
just-indexing-it. If after a few years your picks didn't beat the cheaper, simpler
default, the scorecard just told you something true. Believe it.

The indexer's humility plus the picker's rigor — held at the same time — beats either
religion. The edge is real but rare, so size it like it's rare.

## What I'd Watch

Benchmark your *own* picks honestly: what would you have made just buying the index with
the same money over the same period? If you're not beating that — net of fees, taxes,
and the hours you spent — the index isn't an admission of defeat. It's a raise.
