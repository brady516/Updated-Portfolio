# The "10x stock" teardown

You've seen the video. Confident guy, dramatic thumbnail, an arrow drawn at a
diagonal, a single ticker that's going to **10x**. Today we take one apart — not
to dunk (okay, a little to dunk), but because the questions that turn a pitch into
an analysis are the whole game. They work on *any* bull case, including the one
you're a little too excited about.

*(Generic example. No specific company, security, or creator is named or
recommended — this is a method, not a tip.)*

---

## Signal vs. Noise — anatomy of a 10x pitch *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "This $2B company is the next $200B giant. 100x revenue growth.
> Early. Get in before everyone else."

Here's what's missing from every pitch like that:

- **What has to be true.** A 10x in price isn't a vibe; it's a chain of required
  events. Spell them out and multiply the probabilities — the honest number gets
  small fast.
- **The denominator.** "10x revenue!" from a tiny base is easy and common; it
  rarely survives contact with competition and margins.
- **Dilution.** Hyper-growth companies raise money. Your slice can shrink even if
  the company wins. Price ≠ your return.
- **The risk of zero.** A 10x candidate usually has a real chance of going to ~0.
  A pitch that shows you the upside and hides the left tail isn't analysis.

> **The Signal:** every bull case is a set of *required assumptions* with
> *probabilities* and a *downside*. Write them down. The story survives or it
> doesn't.

## The Number — the probability tax

If a 10x needs five independent things to each go right, and each is 60% likely,
the combined probability is 0.6⁵ ≈ **7.8%**. The pitch sells you the 10x; the math
sells you a ~92% chance it *doesn't* happen as told. Both can be true — just size
accordingly (next week's issue).

## The Read — rebuilding the case with the math it left out *(paid)*

Let's do the honest version on our generic "NextGiant Corp." (Any resemblance to a
stock currently in a YouTube thumbnail is entirely deserved.)

**Step 1 — Reverse the valuation.** Instead of guessing a price target — finance's
favorite party trick — ask what the *current* price already implies. Back into the
revenue, margin, and multiple the market is pricing. The "early" stock has usually
priced in years of flawless execution and a standing ovation.

**Step 2 — Build the required-assumptions table.**

| Assumption | Needed for 10x | Roughly how likely? |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue compounds ~40%/yr for 7 yrs | Yes | Optimistic |
| Gross margin expands to best-in-class | Yes | Uncertain |
| No fatal competitor / regulatory hit | Yes | Coin-flip-ish |
| Share count grows < 25% (limited dilution) | Yes | Doubtful for cash-burners |
| Exit multiple stays premium | Yes | Mean-reversion says no |

Multiply your honest probabilities. That product — not the thumbnail — is your
real base rate.

**Step 3 — Three scenarios, not one.** Bear (capital impaired), base (good
business, ho-hum return), bull (the 10x). Assign probabilities, weight the
outcomes. A position can be worth holding *even with a small 10x chance* — but
only at a size that survives the bear case.

**Step 4 — Pre-commit the downside.** Before buying: what would tell you the
thesis is broken, and what size means a total loss is survivable? Decide it now,
in writing, while you're calm.

**The point:** the teardown doesn't prove the stock is bad. It converts a *story*
into a *bet with known odds and a known worst case*. That's the whole job.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"It's a high-conviction, asymmetric, multi-bagger setup
with a long runway and optionality to the upside."*

**Translation:** "I like it and I think it goes up a lot."

**What it's hiding:** every word doing work here is unfalsifiable. "High
conviction" measures *his* feelings, not the odds. "Asymmetric" and "optionality
to the upside" quietly drop the *downside* — the very thing the required-assumptions
table above forces onto the page. "Long runway" = no timeline you can hold him to.
Strip the seasoning and you're left with an opinion wearing a lab coat.

## What I'd Watch

Take the most exciting position in your own portfolio and build its
required-assumptions table this week. If you can't list what has to be true — or
you flinch at the bear case — that's the signal.
