# How to actually rebalance (and how often)

Rebalancing is the rare free lunch that's also deeply unpleasant to eat. It forces you
to sell what's been winning and buy what's been losing — which is the opposite of every
instinct you have, and exactly why it works. It's also where most written plans quietly
die, because nobody feels like trimming the thing that's going up.

## Signal vs. Noise — "let your winners run" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "Never sell your winners." Said with the confidence of someone in a
> bull market.

There's truth in letting winners run *inside a thesis* — but as a *portfolio* rule it's
how a balanced allocation quietly becomes a single concentrated bet. Do nothing for a
few hot years and your "diversified" portfolio is now 70% one sector you never chose to
overweight. The winner didn't just run; it took over.

> **The Signal:** rebalancing is selling high and buying low, on rules, before emotion
> votes. It feels wrong every single time, which is the proof it's working.

## The Number — drift *(free)*

Set a 60/40 and leave it through a long stock run and you can wake up at 75/25 —
carrying far more risk than you signed up for, right before the risk matters. You didn't
decide to take more risk. Drift decided for you. Rebalancing is how you take the
steering wheel back from the market.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We employ a dynamic, tactical rebalancing framework to
opportunistically optimize risk-adjusted exposures as conditions warrant."*

**Translation:** "We rebalance whenever we feel like it."

**What it's hiding:** "dynamic," "tactical," "opportunistically," and "as conditions
warrant" all mean *discretionary* — i.e., no rule, which means emotion and market-timing
with extra costs. A real rebalancing process is embarrassingly specific and boring:
"when an asset drifts 5% from target, or every January." Specific survives a bad day.
"As conditions warrant" doesn't.

## The Read — a rebalancing rule you'll actually keep *(paid)*

Pick *one* trigger and write it down:

- **Calendar:** rebalance on a set date (annually is plenty for most; quarterly is
  ample). Simple, automatable, no judgment calls.
- **Threshold (bands):** rebalance only when an asset drifts more than X% from its
  target (say ±5 absolute or ±25% relative). Trades less, captures the big dislocations.
- **Hybrid:** check on the calendar, act only if bands are breached. Best of both — most
  of the discipline, least of the churn.

Then the practical layer that keeps it cheap and tax-smart:
- **Rebalance with new money first.** Direct contributions/dividends into the
  *underweight* asset — you rebalance without selling anything (no tax, no spread).
- **Mind the account.** Do the taxable-event-heavy rebalancing inside tax-sheltered
  accounts where you can (see Issue #18 on tax drag).
- **Don't over-trade it.** More frequent ≠ better; you bleed costs and taxes chasing
  precision the market doesn't reward. Boring cadence beats busy.

The point isn't optimization to the basis point. It's having a rule that makes the
hard, correct trade *for* you, on a day you'd never choose to make it yourself.

## What I'd Watch

Check your current mix against your target. If it's drifted hard, you're not running
the allocation you chose — you're running the one the last bull market built. Decide
your trigger (date or band), write it on the one-page plan from Issue #12, and let the
rule do the flinching so you don't have to.
