# Estate basics: the stuff nobody wants to do

This is the issue people skip, which is exactly why it matters. You can do everything
else right — build the wealth, dodge the fees, name your "enough" — and still hand your
family a legal mess and a tax bill because you couldn't face an afternoon of paperwork.
Estate planning isn't about being rich. It's about not leaving a problem.

## Signal vs. Noise — "estate planning is for rich people" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "I don't have an estate — that's a rich-person thing."

If you have *any* assets, a bank account, a retirement plan, a kid, or a body that
needs medical decisions made — you have an estate and you need a plan. Without one, the
*state* decides who gets what (intestacy) and who raises your children, on a timeline
and in a courtroom your family will not enjoy.

> **The Signal:** estate planning is *deciding in advance* — who gets what, who decides
> for you, who raises your kids — so a court and a tax code don't decide worse.

## The Number — the beneficiary override *(free)*

Here's the one almost everyone gets wrong: **beneficiary designations on retirement
accounts and life insurance OVERRIDE your will.** A 401(k) still listing an ex-spouse
goes to the ex — your will is irrelevant. Five minutes checking beneficiaries is the
single highest-leverage estate task there is, and the most commonly botched.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week — Estate Edition *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our holistic, multi-generational wealth-transfer strategy
leverages sophisticated vehicles to optimize your legacy across the family enterprise."*

**Translation:** "We'll sell you complicated trusts and probably some insurance."

**What it's hiding:** "sophisticated vehicles" and "family enterprise" inflate ordinary
people's needs into a product suite — often pairing complex trusts with commissioned
insurance (Issue #25). Most people need a *will, beneficiaries, powers of attorney, and
maybe one simple trust* — not a "multi-generational" architecture sized to justify a fee.

## The Read — the actual checklist *(paid)*

The documents most people genuinely need (this is a map, not legal advice — see a
qualified estate attorney):

- **A will** — who gets your assets and, critically, **who is guardian of your minor
  children.** No will = the court decides both.
- **Beneficiary designations** — on retirement accounts, IRAs, life insurance.
  Check them, and check them again after every marriage, divorce, birth, or death.
  *They override the will.*
- **Durable power of attorney (financial)** — someone who can handle money if you're
  incapacitated, so the family avoids a court conservatorship.
- **Healthcare directive / medical POA / living will** — who makes medical decisions and
  what your wishes are. This is for *you while alive,* not just after.
- **A revocable living trust — sometimes.** Useful to **avoid probate** (the public,
  slow, costly court process) and to control *how/when* heirs receive assets (e.g., not
  a lump sum to a 19-year-old). Genuinely helpful for many; not mandatory for everyone.
  Don't let it be upsold as a panacea.
- **A "where everything is" letter** — accounts, passwords, the attorney's name, the
  insurance policies. The kindest, simplest document, and the one nobody writes.

Two honest notes: most estates are **well under the federal estate-tax exemption,** so
for most people this is about *avoiding mess and probate,* not dodging estate tax — be
skeptical of complexity sold on tax fears that don't apply to you. And **review it after
life changes;** a plan naming the wrong people is sometimes worse than none.

It's an unpleasant afternoon that spares your family their worst week. That's the whole
pitch, and it's enough.

## What I'd Watch

Do the five-minute version *today:* log in and check the **beneficiaries** on every
retirement account and insurance policy. If any name surprises you — an ex, a deceased
relative, a blank — you just found a real problem that your will can't fix. Then book the
attorney for the rest.
