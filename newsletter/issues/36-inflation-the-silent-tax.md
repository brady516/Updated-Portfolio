# Inflation: the silent tax on cash and bonds

*Monthly flagship — the long one.*

Inflation is the tax nobody votes for and everybody pays. It doesn't show up on a
statement, it never sends a bill, and it quietly transfers wealth from savers to
borrowers while everyone argues about the nominal number going up. Understand it and a
lot of "safe" suddenly looks risky, and a lot of "risky" looks necessary.

## Signal vs. Noise — "my savings are safe in cash" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "Cash can't lose value — it's the safe choice."

Cash can't lose *nominal* value, which is a different thing from *real* value. At 3%
inflation, money in a 0.5% account loses ~2.5% of its *purchasing power* every year —
guaranteed, silently. "Safe" against price swings, "guaranteed loss" against the cost
of living. Both true; only one gets said out loud.

> **The Signal:** judge everything in *real* (after-inflation) terms. Nominal numbers
> are the brochure; real numbers are the truth.

## The Number — the rule of 72, in reverse *(free)*

Run the rule of 72 on inflation: at **3%**, prices double — and a dollar's purchasing
power halves — in about **24 years.** At **6%**, twelve years. The retiree who plans in
today's dollars and ignores this is planning for a future that costs roughly *double.*
Inflation is slow enough to ignore and relentless enough to ruin you.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our capital-preservation strategy prioritizes principal
protection through high-quality fixed income and cash equivalents."*

**Translation:** "We'll keep your nominal dollars and let inflation eat them."

**What it's hiding:** "capital preservation" is defined in *nominal* terms — it
preserves the *number,* not the *purchasing power.* For a long horizon, an all-cash/
short-bond "safe" portfolio is a near-certain *real* loss. The word "preservation"
quietly drops the only adjective that matters: *real.*

## The Read — what actually defends purchasing power *(paid)*

No perfect inflation hedge exists; the honest answer is a *mix* that, over time, tends
to outpace it:

- **Equities (the long-run workhorse).** Owning real businesses that can raise prices
  has historically beaten inflation over long periods — volatile year to year, but the
  primary real-growth engine. The "risk" of stocks and the "safety" of cash trade
  places once you measure in *real* terms over decades.
- **TIPS / I-Bonds.** Treasury inflation-protected securities and I-Bonds adjust with
  CPI by design — a direct, if modest, hedge for the conservative slice.
- **Real assets** (real estate, sometimes commodities/infrastructure) — imperfect,
  but tend to move with the price level over long stretches.
- **Where inflation *wins*:** long nominal bonds and idle cash. Fine for *short-term*
  needs and the emergency fund (Issue #30); corrosive as a *long-term* home for wealth.

The trap is the mismatch: people park *long-term* money in *nominally-safe* assets
because the statement never shows a loss — while inflation drains it the whole time.
The deeper point: **"safe" and "risky" swap meaning depending on your horizon.** For
money you need next year, cash is safe and stocks are risky. For money you need in
thirty years, *cash is the risky bet* — it's the one almost guaranteed to lose real
ground — and owning productive assets is the prudent one. Match the asset to the
horizon, in real terms, and inflation stops being a silent thief.

## What I'd Watch

Take your "safe" money and ask: *what's its real return — yield minus inflation?* If
it's negative and that's money you won't need for years, "safe" is quietly costing you.
Short-term reserves belong in cash; long-term wealth parked in cash is the silent tax's
favorite meal.
