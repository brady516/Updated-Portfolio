# Liquidity: the risk nobody prices until it's too late

Most risk gets a number. Volatility, beta, drawdown — all measured, all on the
dashboard. Liquidity is the one that hides, costs nothing to ignore for years, and
then bills you the entire account in a single bad week. It's the risk of not being
able to get *out* at a price that resembles the one on your screen.

## Signal vs. Noise — "it's only a paper loss" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "I'm not selling, so the markdown doesn't matter."

That logic only holds if you can *choose* when you sell. Liquidity risk is precisely
the scenario where the choice gets taken from you — a margin call, a redemption, a
life event — and you're forced to sell the thing nobody wants to buy, at their price.

> **The Signal:** ask not "what's it worth?" but "what could I actually sell it for,
> today, in size, in a panic?" The gap between those two numbers is your real risk.

## The Number — the bid/ask in a storm *(free)*

In calm markets a liquid stock's spread is pennies. In a crisis, spreads on
thin names blow out — 5%, 10%, "no bid" — at the exact moment everyone wants out the
same door. Liquidity isn't a constant you can assume; it's a fair-weather friend that
leaves when you need it.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"The fund may invest in less-liquid instruments that, under
certain market conditions, could be difficult to value or dispose of in a timely
manner."*

**Translation:** "Some of what we own can't be sold fast, and in a crash, maybe not
at all."

**What it's hiding:** this sentence is buried in the prospectus on purpose — it's the
most honest line in the document and the least read. "Difficult to dispose of in a
timely manner" is regulatory poetry for "you're trapped."

## The Read — pricing the thing nobody prices *(paid)*

- **Know your exit before your entry.** Average daily volume, typical spread, how many
  days it'd take to exit your position without moving the price. If the answer is
  "weeks," that's a position-size limit, not a footnote.
- **Liquidity correlates to zero when you need it.** It evaporates across everything
  at once in a panic — the diversification you counted on and the exit you counted on
  fail in the same hour.
- **Match liquidity to obligations.** Money you might *need* belongs in things you can
  *sell* — cash, T-bills, large liquid names. Illiquidity (private deals, thin
  small-caps, locked vehicles) is fine *only* with money you can genuinely leave alone.
- **The illiquidity premium is real — and a trap if forced.** You can get paid to hold
  what others can't easily sell. You can also become the forced seller who pays that
  premium to someone else. Which one you are is decided entirely by your cash needs.

## What I'd Watch

Look at your least-liquid holding and ask: if I *had* to raise cash next month, what
would dumping this actually fetch? If you don't know, you don't know your risk — you
know your *quote.* Those are different numbers, and the difference shows up at the
worst possible time.
