# Covered calls: income, with a catch

"Free monthly income from stocks you already own" is the pitch. Covered calls are a
real, useful strategy — and that sentence is still missing the catch big enough to
park a truck in. You're not conjuring income; you're **selling your upside** for a
premium. Sometimes that's a great trade. Knowing *when* is the whole job.

## Signal vs. Noise — "free money every month" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** sell calls, collect premium, repeat, retire. The thumbnail has a
> beach.

There's no free money. The premium is payment for a real thing you gave away: the
gains above your strike. In a ripping market, you'll watch your stock get called
away and "miss" the move you were there for. That's not a glitch — it's the deal.

> **The Signal:** covered calls trade *upside* for *income and a little downside
> cushion.* Fair trade in flat-to-mildly-up markets; a bad trade right before a melt-up.

## The Number — annualize it honestly *(free)*

A "2% premium this month" is not 2%. Annualized and net of the upside you capped, the
real return depends entirely on what the stock does. The premium is the *certain*
part; the *opportunity cost* is the part the pitch quietly omits.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"An income-enhancement overlay designed to monetize
volatility and generate consistent yield."*

**Translation:** "We sell call options."

**What it's hiding:** "monetize volatility" sounds like alchemy; it's renting out
your upside. "Consistent yield" right until a big up-month caps you out or a crash
blows through your tiny premium cushion. Useful tool, oversold noun.

## The Read — when the trade actually makes sense *(paid)*

- **Your outlook is flat-to-slightly-up.** That's the sweet spot — you collect
  premium and probably keep the shares.
- **You'd be content to sell at the strike.** Treat the strike as a price you'd
  happily exit at, because you might. If you'd be gutted to lose the shares, don't
  write the call.
- **Liquid options, sane strikes.** Wide spreads eat the premium; greedy strikes
  cap you right where the gains live.
- **Mind the tax and assignment mechanics.** Getting called away can trigger gains
  you didn't plan for. The "income" isn't free of consequences either.

## What I'd Watch

Next time you see "X% monthly income from options," ask the one question the pitch
skips: *what happens in a big up-month?* If the answer is "I cap out and miss it,"
you now understand covered calls better than the person selling the course on them.
