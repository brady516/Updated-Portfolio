# Reading an analyst price target for what it really is

"Analyst sets $250 price target" is one of the most-quoted, least-useful headlines in
finance. A price target looks like a precise forecast from a professional. It's
closer to a marketing number with a model attached — and once you know how they're
made and what incentives shape them, you'll read them very differently.

## Signal vs. Noise — "Wall Street says $250" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** the target as a prediction. "Analysts have a $250 target, so there's
> 25% upside."

A 12-month price target is one analyst's single-point guess, built on assumptions that
move with the wind, and shaped by incentives that mostly point *up.* Treating it as a
forecast is treating a guess in a tie as a fact.

> **The Signal:** the target is *output,* not insight. The *assumptions* behind it —
> and the *direction of revisions* — carry the information. The number itself is theater.

## The Number — the ratings tilt *(free)*

Sell-side ratings skew overwhelmingly to **Buy/Hold; outright Sells are rare.** Why?
Banks have relationships with the companies they cover, and "Sell" burns access and
deal flow. So the *base rate* of optimism is structural. Read every target through that
lens: the thumb is on the bullish side of the scale before the model even runs.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We are raising our price target to $250 (from $220) on
improved visibility and multiple re-rating potential, reiterating our Overweight."*

**Translation:** "The stock went up, so we raised our number to match."

**What it's hiding:** targets often *follow* the price rather than lead it — a rising
stock gets a rising target so the analyst isn't caught looking wrong. "Multiple
re-rating potential" means "we're assuming people will pay more for the same earnings,"
which is a hope, not an analysis. The *upgrade after the move* is the tell.

## The Read — what's actually worth mining *(paid)*

- **Ignore the number; read the model.** What revenue growth, margin, and *multiple*
  does the target assume? Run it as a reverse-DCF (Issue #4): is it pricing the obvious
  or a miracle? The assumptions are checkable; the headline isn't.
- **Watch revisions, not levels.** The *direction and clustering* of estimate changes
  across analysts carries more signal than any single target — a wave of upward
  earnings revisions means something; one bank's round number doesn't.
- **Mind the incentives.** Coverage relationships, banking ties, and access pressure
  bias toward optimism. A rare *downgrade* often carries more information than a tenth
  Buy, precisely because it cost the analyst something.
- **Separate estimate from target.** *Earnings estimates* (what the company will earn)
  are more useful than *price targets* (what the analyst guesses the market will pay).
  The first is analysis; the second adds a multiple guess on top.
- **Use them as a research index, not an answer.** The best use of analyst work is the
  *homework* in the report — the segment detail, the model build — not the conclusion
  on the cover.

A price target isn't useless; it's just not what it looks like. Mine the assumptions,
track the revisions, respect the incentives, and ignore the round number on the
headline.

## What I'd Watch

Next "Bank raises target to $X" you see, ask two things: *did the target rise because
the business improved, or because the stock already did?* and *what multiple is baked
in?* If it's price-chasing on an expanding multiple, you're reading momentum dressed as
analysis.
