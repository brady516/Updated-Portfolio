# The psychology of "enough"

*Monthly flagship — the long one, and the most important one I'll write.*

Every other issue is about getting the numbers right. This one's about the question the
numbers can't answer: *how much is enough?* It's the only finance question that, left
unanswered, quietly ruins people who "win" — the ones who hit the number and keep
playing until they give it back, or hit it and never feel it. The math is the easy part.
Knowing when to stop is the whole game.

## Signal vs. Noise — "more is always better" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** the entire industry's background hum — *more.* More return, more
> growth, more, forever. A number is never named, because a named number ends the sale.

Without a definition of *enough,* "more" is a treadmill with no off switch — you risk
what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need. The people who blow up
are rarely the ones who lacked money; they're the ones who never decided how much was
plenty.

> **The Signal:** define *enough* — an actual number and an actual life — *before* the
> market or your own ego defines it for you. The goal isn't maximum; it's *sufficient,
> then free.*

## The Number — the one you have to name yourself *(free)*

There's no formula here, and that's the point. Roughly: **enough = the assets that
fund the life you actually want, plus a real margin of safety.** Most people have never
once calculated it — so they chase a moving target set by comparison and marketing,
and "rich" stays permanently one rung up. Name your number and the target stops moving.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We help clients maximize their wealth and optimize their
financial potential to achieve their fullest growth trajectory."*

**Translation:** "We want your money invested, maximally, indefinitely."

**What it's hiding:** "maximize," "optimize," "fullest potential" — none of it ever
names a *finish line,* because the business model is *assets under management forever.*
An industry paid on how much you have will never tell you when you have enough. That
sentence has to come from you.

## The Read — finding (and defending) your enough *(paid)*

- **Name the number.** What annual spending funds the life you genuinely want? Multiply
  for a safe withdrawal (Issue #28), add margin. That's "enough" — a finish line, in
  dollars, that you choose.
- **Separate enough from more.** Below enough, optimize hard — the marginal dollar
  matters enormously. *Above* enough, the marginal dollar buys very little happiness and
  often costs you risk, time, and peace. The whole error is using *below-enough*
  intensity on *above-enough* money.
- **Beware the ratchet.** Lifestyle creep moves "enough" up as fast as income — so the
  number is never reached. Defining enough means *fixing* the target, not letting each
  raise quietly redefine it.
- **The real risk above enough is ruin, not missing out.** Once you've won the game,
  the catastrophic mistake is taking the risk that gives it back (Issue #3, #26, #47).
  "He risked what he had and needed to get what he didn't have and didn't need" is the
  saddest sentence in finance — and the most common.
- **Convert money into the things it's for.** Time, freedom, health, people, work you'd
  do anyway. Money is the means; "enough" is the moment you start spending it on the
  end. The point of the creed — *never outsource your wealth* (Issue #15) — is so that
  *you* get to decide when it's done, not a system that profits from "never."

The cruelest outcome in investing isn't being poor. It's being someone who had enough,
didn't know it, and kept playing until the market or their ego took it back. Don't be
the cautionary tale who won and didn't notice.

## What I'd Watch

Answer the question almost no one does: *what is my number, and what is the life on the
other side of it?* If you can't say it, you can't aim at it — and "more" will keep being
the answer to a question you never actually asked. Name your enough. Everything else in
this newsletter is just how to get there efficiently.
