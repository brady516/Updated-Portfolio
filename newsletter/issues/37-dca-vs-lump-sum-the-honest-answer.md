# Dollar-cost averaging vs. lump sum: the honest answer

You just got a windfall — inheritance, bonus, a house sale. Do you invest it all now
(lump sum) or feed it in over months (dollar-cost averaging)? The internet will yell
both answers at you with equal confidence. The honest answer is mildly annoying: the
*math* favors one and the *human* often needs the other, and pretending otherwise helps
nobody.

## Signal vs. Noise — "DCA always reduces risk" *(free)*

> **The Noise (one camp):** "Always DCA — it lowers your risk and your average cost."
> **The Noise (other camp):** "DCA is mathematically dumb, always lump sum."

Both overclaim. DCA doesn't reliably lower *cost* (markets rise more often than they
fall, so waiting usually means buying higher). What it lowers is *regret* — the
specific agony of dumping it all in the day before a crash.

> **The Signal:** lump sum wins on *expected return;* DCA wins on *emotional
> survivability.* Pick based on which failure would actually break *your* plan.

## The Number — markets are up more than down *(free)*

Because markets rise the majority of the time, **lump-sum investing beats DCA roughly
two-thirds of the time,** and by a meaningful margin on average. Time *in* the market
beats timing your *entry.* But that's the *average* — the third of the time DCA wins, it
wins precisely when you'd most regret a lump sum (right before a drop), which is exactly
when people panic and abandon the plan.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We recommend a disciplined, phased deployment strategy to
strategically average into the market and mitigate timing risk."*

**Translation:** "We'll DCA your money in, which also keeps you from noticing fees on a
big balance all at once."

**What it's hiding:** "phased deployment to mitigate timing risk" sounds like prudence
and is often just *DCA with a fee.* It can be the right behavioral call — but notice
it's framed as risk-reduction when the data says it usually reduces *return.* The honest
version names the trade-off; the salad only names the upside.

## The Read — choosing like a grown-up *(paid)*

- **If you can stomach it: lump sum.** The expected-value answer is "invest it now, per
  your target allocation." Most of the time, most of the upside, you keep.
- **If a near-term crash would make you bail: DCA — on a fixed schedule.** Splitting it
  into, say, equal chunks over 3–12 months trades a little expected return for a lot of
  *staying power.* A plan you'll actually stick to beats a superior plan you'll abandon
  in a panic. That's not weakness; it's risk management of your own behavior (Issue #8).
- **Make it rules-based, not mood-based.** If you DCA, pre-commit the schedule and
  *follow it* — including buying into a scary dip. DCA that becomes "I'll wait for things
  to calm down" is just market-timing with a respectable name, and it's how people end
  up holding cash for two years (Issue #36 — where inflation eats it).
- **Match it to the money's job.** Long-horizon money tilts toward lump sum; money you
  can't emotionally afford to see drop tilts toward DCA — or maybe toward a more
  conservative *allocation* in the first place, which is the better lever anyway.

The thing nobody admits: **the best strategy is the one you'll actually execute.** A
mathematically optimal lump sum you panic-sell in month two is worse than a slightly
suboptimal DCA you ride for thirty years. Know which version of you is showing up, and
plan for *that* one.

## What I'd Watch

Got a windfall? Ask yourself one honest question: *if I invest it all today and the
market drops 20% next month, do I stay the course or do I bail?* If "stay" — lump sum.
If "bail" — DCA on a fixed schedule (and maybe dial down the allocation). The right
answer isn't in a study; it's in your honest read of your own nerve.
