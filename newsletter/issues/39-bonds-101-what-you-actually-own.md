# Bonds 101: what you actually own when you "own bonds"

Most people hold bonds because someone told them to, and couldn't tell you what a bond
*is* if you asked. That's fine until the year bonds *fall* — and then "the safe part of
my portfolio" doing the opposite of safe sends everyone into a panic they could've
avoided by understanding one or two mechanics. Let's fix that.

## Signal vs. Noise — "bonds are the safe part" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "bonds = safe, stocks = risky," full stop.

Bonds carry their *own* risks — interest-rate risk (price falls when rates rise),
credit risk (the borrower may not pay), and inflation risk (fixed payments lose real
value). 2022 taught a generation that a "safe" long-bond fund can drop double digits.
Bonds are *different* risk from stocks, not *no* risk.

> **The Signal:** a bond is a **loan you made,** with three questions — *who's
> borrowing, for how long, and what does that do to the price if rates move?*

## The Number — par, coupon, yield *(free)*

A bond pays a fixed **coupon** on its face value and returns the **par** at maturity.
Its **price moves opposite to rates:** rates up → existing bonds (paying the old, lower
coupon) are worth less; rates down → worth more. How *much* it moves is **duration**
(Issue #11): duration 7 ≈ a 7% price drop per 1% rate rise. That single relationship
explains 90% of "why did my safe bonds fall?"

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We favor a barbell duration posture with an up-in-quality
bias to capture carry while managing convexity in the current rate regime."*

**Translation:** "We own some short and some long bonds, mostly safe issuers, for the
yield."

**What it's hiding:** nothing sinister — it's just gatekeeping vocabulary. "Carry" =
the yield you earn for holding. "Up-in-quality" = safer borrowers. The jargon makes a
simple set of choices sound like a priesthood, which is exactly how simple products get
sold at complex prices.

## The Read — the parts that actually matter *(paid)*

- **Who's borrowing (credit risk).** Treasuries (US government, essentially default-
  free), investment-grade corporates (safe-ish companies), high-yield/"junk" (riskier
  companies, fatter coupons, behaves more like stocks in a crisis). Higher yield is
  pay for higher risk — always ask *why* the yield is high.
- **For how long (rate risk / duration).** Short bonds barely move with rates but pay
  less; long bonds pay more but swing hard. Match duration to when you need the money
  (Issue #16).
- **Bond vs. bond *fund*.** Hold an *individual* bond to maturity and (barring default)
  you get par back regardless of price swings. A bond *fund* never matures — it's a
  rolling portfolio whose price genuinely falls and stays down if rates rose. Different
  experiences from the "same" asset.
- **Why own them at all.** Income, lower volatility than stocks, and *diversification* —
  bonds often (not always) zig when stocks zag. The job of bonds in most portfolios is
  ballast and dry powder, not maximum return.
- **The real risks, named:** rates (duration), default (credit), and the quiet one —
  **inflation** eating fixed payments (Issue #36). "Safe" nominal, not always safe real.

You don't need the jargon. You need: *who am I lending to, for how long, and what
happens to the price if rates move?* Answer those and you understand your bonds better
than most people who own a target-date fund full of them.

## What I'd Watch

Find the **duration and credit quality** of your bond holdings (your fund's page lists
both). If you're surprised by either — a long duration you didn't know you had, or
"high-yield" you thought was safe — that's the gap between what you own and what you
*think* you own. Close it before the next rate surprise does it for you.
