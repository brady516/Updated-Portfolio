# Factor investing without the marketing

"Factors" — value, quality, momentum, size, low-volatility — are real, decades-deep
findings about what has historically driven returns. They're also the most
academic-sounding thing the fund industry has ever turned into a product line, which
means the real research and the marketing wearing its lab coat are now hard to tell
apart. Let's separate them.

## Signal vs. Noise — "smart beta" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "smart beta" — the label itself implies regular indexing is *dumb*
> beta, and that for a higher fee you get the clever version.

Some factors have genuine, long-run, multi-country evidence behind them. But "factor"
also became a marketing wrapper to charge index-plus fees for index-ish products, and
to launch a new "factor fund" right after that factor had a hot run (and is due to cool).

> **The Signal:** factors are real *tendencies,* not switches — they work on average,
> over long horizons, with stretches of painful underperformance that are the whole
> reason the premium might persist.

## The Number — the decade of doubt *(free)*

A real factor can *underperform for 10+ years* and still be "working." Value famously
trailed for much of the 2010s. That's not a bug — if a factor were comfortable to hold,
everyone would, and the premium would vanish. The discomfort *is* the supposed source
of the reward, which is exactly why most people bail at the bottom.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our multi-factor strategy dynamically tilts toward
academically-validated return premia to enhance risk-adjusted performance."*

**Translation:** "We weight the index differently and charge more."

**What it's hiding:** "dynamically tilts" can mean *factor timing* — jumping between
factors — which the evidence says is extremely hard and mostly subtracts value after
costs. "Academically-validated" is true of the *factors* and not necessarily of *this
fund's* implementation, fees, or timing.

## The Read — using factors honestly *(paid)*

- **Know which factors have real legs.** The most-evidenced: value (cheap beats dear),
  quality (profitable, stable firms), momentum (recent winners persist short-term),
  size (small > large, weakly), low-volatility (boring wins risk-adjusted). Even these
  are debated and weaker net of costs than the backtests promise (Issue #10).
- **Tilt, don't bet the farm.** A factor tilt is a *seasoning* on a broad-market core,
  not a replacement for it. If a "factor fund" is really a concentrated active bet,
  price it as one.
- **Check the fee against plain index.** If "smart beta" costs 0.40% vs. 0.04% for the
  total market, the factor has to overcome a 0.36%/yr head start *forever* (Issue #14).
  Often it doesn't.
- **Mind implementation and timing.** *Which* value metric, *how* it's rebalanced,
  *what* it costs to trade momentum — the details decide whether the premium survives
  contact with reality. And factor-*timing* (chasing whatever's hot) is where good
  intentions go to underperform.
- **Hold long enough to matter, or don't bother.** A factor you abandon during its
  inevitable bad decade was a fee you paid to underperform twice. If you can't commit
  through the pain, the premium isn't yours to collect.

Factors aren't snake oil and they aren't magic. They're real, modest, uncomfortable
edges — worth a measured tilt for the patient, and a marketing trap for the impatient
paying up to rent last decade's winner.

## What I'd Watch

If you own a "factor" or "smart beta" fund, ask: *which factor, what does it cost vs.
the plain index, and would I hold it through a decade of underperformance?* If you
can't answer all three, you don't own a strategy — you own a brochure with a tilt.
