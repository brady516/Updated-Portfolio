# The exit nobody teaches

*Monthly flagship — the long one.*

There are ten thousand guides on what to buy and almost none on when to sell. That's
backwards, because selling is where the money is actually made or lost — and it's the
hardest thing you'll do, because every selling decision is an ambush by your own
psychology. You can be a brilliant buyer and a poor seller and end up mediocre. Most
people do.

## Signal vs. Noise — "I'll just know when to sell" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "I'll sell when it feels right / when I've made enough / when it tops."

You won't "just know," because the exact moments selling matters are the moments your
brain is least reliable — euphoria at the top, panic at the bottom. "Feel right" is how
people ride winners to round-trips and dump good assets into crashes.

> **The Signal:** decide your sell rules *before* you're emotional — in writing, at
> purchase — so the calm version of you instructs the panicked one (Issue #12).

## The Number — the round-trip *(free)*

The most common wealth-destroyer isn't picking a loser; it's letting a *winner* become
everything and then giving the whole gain back because you never had a trim rule. Paper
gains feel like money. They aren't money until you sell — and "I was up 300% once" is a
sentence people say about positions they rode all the way back down.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We maintain a long-term conviction and view recent weakness as
a compelling opportunity to average down into our highest-conviction names."*

**Translation:** "It's falling and we're buying more to feel better about being wrong."

**What it's hiding:** "averaging down into conviction" is sometimes correct and
sometimes just *refusing to admit a broken thesis* — and the phrase can't tell you
which. The honest test isn't conviction; it's whether the *reason you bought* still
holds. "Long-term conviction" is often the costume a sunk-cost fallacy wears.

## The Read — building an exit before you need one *(paid)*

Write these at *purchase,* when you're calm:

1. **The thesis, in one sentence.** "I own this because X." You sell when **X breaks** —
   not when the price wobbles, and not never. The thesis breaking is the cleanest sell
   signal there is.
2. **A trim rule for winners.** When a position grows past some % of your portfolio
   (say it doubles its target weight), trim back to target. You're not calling the top;
   you're refusing to let one bet quietly become your whole net worth (Issues #3, #27).
3. **A downside line.** Not a twitchy stop, but a pre-decided "if it falls to here *and*
   the thesis is impaired, I'm out." Decide what *impaired* means in advance.
4. **A "would I buy it today?" check.** Periodically re-underwrite each holding as if you
   held cash. If you wouldn't buy it here, the only reason you're holding is inertia or
   attachment — neither is a thesis.
5. **Separate the *asset* decision from the *tax* decision.** Don't let "I don't want to
   pay the gain" trap you in a position you'd otherwise exit (Issue #18) — but do let tax
   *timing* inform *how* you exit.

The enemies you're defending against: **anchoring** (to your cost basis), **loss
aversion** (selling winners early, riding losers down), **sunk cost** ("I've held this
forever"), and **endowment** (it feels more valuable because it's *yours*). Naming them
is half the defense; a written rule is the other half.

Buying is optimism, which is easy. Selling is judgment under fire, which is why nobody
teaches it and everybody needs it.

## What I'd Watch

Pick any holding and try to state, right now, *what would make you sell it.* If you
can't — no thesis-break, no trim rule, no downside line — you don't own an investment
with an exit. You own a position you'll eventually sell on emotion, at the time emotion
is worst. Write the exit down today.
