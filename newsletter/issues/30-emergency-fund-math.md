# Emergency fund math: how much, and where

The least glamorous holding in your whole financial life is also the one that decides
whether a bad month becomes a catastrophe. The emergency fund isn't an *investment* —
it's the thing that stops you from becoming a *forced seller* of your investments
(Issue #16) at the worst possible time. Boring, unsexy, and the foundation everything
else stands on.

## Signal vs. Noise — "cash is trash" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "Cash loses to inflation — keep everything invested and put it to work."

True for *long-term* money. Catastrophically wrong for *emergency* money. The point of
an emergency fund isn't return; it's **certainty and access.** The day you need it,
"my emergency fund is up 9%" is irrelevant and "it's down 30% and I have to sell at
the bottom" is a disaster.

> **The Signal:** the emergency fund has one job — *be there, in full, instantly,* when
> income stops or a surprise hits. Judge it on safety and access, never on yield.

## The Number — 3 to 6 months (but it depends) *(free)*

The rule of thumb is **3–6 months of essential expenses** — but the right number is
personal: closer to **3** if you have stable dual income and a safety net; closer to
**6–12** if you're single-income, self-employed, commission-based, or your job market
is thin. Size it to *how long your income could realistically vanish,* not a slogan.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Optimize your idle cash with our liquid, high-yield cash
management solution offering competitive, risk-adjusted returns."*

**Translation:** "We'd like to manage your savings account."

**What it's hiding:** "idle cash" frames your *safety reserve* as a problem to be
"optimized" — the exact mindset that talks people into making their emergency fund less
safe or less accessible chasing a little yield. Some yield is fine (see below); the
tell is any pitch that treats your foundation as money "not working hard enough."

## The Read — sizing it and parking it *(paid)*

**How much:**
- Base it on **essential** monthly costs (housing, food, utilities, insurance,
  minimum debt payments), not your full lifestyle spend.
- Multiply by your realistic income-gap risk: stable → 3–4 months; variable/single
  income/self-employed → 6–12.
- Add a line for likely *lumpy* surprises (deductibles, a transmission) on top.

**Where to keep it (safe + accessible, with a little yield is fine):**
- **High-yield savings / money market fund** — FDIC-insured savings or a government
  money-market fund pays a real rate now *without* sacrificing access. This is the
  sweet spot: safe, liquid, earns something.
- **T-bills / a short-term Treasury ladder** for a portion if the fund is large —
  safe, liquid-ish, sometimes better after-tax (no state tax on Treasuries).
- **Not** in stocks, crypto, your brokerage's risky sweep, or anything with a lock-up,
  penalty, or price that can drop. The job is certainty, not optimization.

The mindset that matters: this money is *insurance you're self-funding.* You're not
trying to win with it; you're making sure you never have to *lose* with the rest of
your portfolio because life surprised you. A boring, full, accessible emergency fund is
what lets the *aggressive* part of your plan actually stay aggressive through a rough
patch.

## What I'd Watch

Two questions: *How many months of essentials could I cover today, and could I get to
the money this afternoon without selling something at a loss or paying a penalty?* If
either answer is shaky, your foundation is — and that's the first thing to fix, before
any clever investing.
