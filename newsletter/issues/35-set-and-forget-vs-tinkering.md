# Set-and-forget vs. tinkering: when to leave it alone

There are two ways to wreck a good plan: never touch it when it needs maintenance, or
touch it constantly when it needs to be left alone. Most people swing between the two —
neglect in calm times, frantic tinkering in scary ones — which is exactly backward. The
skill is knowing the short list of things worth doing, and treating everything else as
a temptation to resist.

## Signal vs. Noise — "are you even managing this?" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** activity as diligence. The advisor who trades a lot to look busy; the
> app that pings you to *do something;* the feeling that good investors are always
> adjusting.

For a long-term portfolio, **most action is value-destruction with extra steps** —
costs, taxes, and badly-timed moves (Issue #8). The market pays the patient and bills
the busy. Tinkering *feels* like control and usually *is* the leak.

> **The Signal:** a good plan is mostly *maintenance, not management.* A few scheduled
> actions a year; otherwise, hands off — especially when it's scary.

## The Number — the cost of touching it *(free)*

Every "adjustment" can carry a spread, a commission, a tax bill, and a timing error.
Studies of investor behavior keep finding the same thing: the *more* people trade, the
*worse* they tend to do. The portfolio you check daily and tweak monthly almost always
loses to the identical one you rebalanced once a year and otherwise ignored.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"In this environment, we're being highly active and tactical,
continuously repositioning to navigate the volatility."*

**Translation:** "We're trading a lot because doing nothing is hard to bill for."

**What it's hiding:** "highly active" and "continuously repositioning" describe *cost
and market-timing,* dressed as diligence. In volatile markets the value-add is usually
*not* flinching — but "we held steady per your plan" is a hard thing to charge a
performance fee for, so it becomes "tactical navigation."

## The Read — the short list worth doing (everything else: leave it) *(paid)*

**Worth doing (rarely, on schedule):**
- **Rebalance** on your rule (date or band — Issue #21). That's *most* of legitimate
  "management."
- **Add money** on a schedule into the underweight asset.
- **Tax-loss harvest** when a real opportunity appears (Issue #18).
- **Re-underwrite holdings** periodically — has any thesis broken (Issue #34)?
- **Update the plan when your *life* changes** — marriage, kid, job, nearing retirement
  (Issue #28). React to *your* changes, not the market's noise.

**Temptations to resist:**
- Reacting to headlines, "this time is different" (Issue #26), or a scary week.
- Performance-chasing into last year's winner.
- "Optimizing" a fine allocation into a slightly different fine allocation, taxably.
- Checking daily — the more you look, the more you'll feel you must *act.*

The genuinely hard part is that **the right move during turmoil is usually the
*scheduled* one, not a new one.** "Set and forget" doesn't mean *never* maintain — that
neglect lets drift and broken theses fester. It means *maintain on rules and ignore the
rest,* and never let a frightening market promote you from investor to day-trader. Build
the plan so the correct action is almost always "follow the plan."

## What I'd Watch

Look at your last year of portfolio moves. How many were *scheduled maintenance*
(rebalance, contributions, harvesting) versus *reactions* (a headline, a scary day, a
hot tip)? If reactions outnumber maintenance, the leak isn't your strategy — it's your
hands. The fix is a rule that does the few right things and forbids the rest.
