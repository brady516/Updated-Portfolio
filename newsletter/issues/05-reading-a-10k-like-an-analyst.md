# Reading a 10-K like an analyst

The 10-K is the one document a company is legally on the hook for, written by
people who'd rather you not read it closely. Which is exactly why you should. The
good news: you don't read all 180 pages. You read five parts and the footnotes that
bite.

## Signal vs. Noise — "I don't read filings, I read charts" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** the chart is the analysis. Lines go up, lines go down, draw a
> triangle, collect engagement.

A chart tells you what already happened to the price. A filing tells you what's
happening to the *business* — and occasionally what management is hoping you'll skim
past. One of those is research.

> **The Signal:** read the boring document the company's lawyers sweated over. The
> price is an opinion; the 10-K is closer to a confession.

## The Number — Item 1A *(free)*

The "Risk Factors" section (Item 1A) is where companies list, in their own words,
everything that could go wrong — because not listing it is a lawsuit. Most of it is
boilerplate; the **new** risk language year-over-year is where the bodies are.
Compare this year's to last year's and read what changed.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Results were impacted by certain non-recurring items and
unfavorable timing, partially offset by continued operational discipline."*

**Translation:** "We had a bad quarter and we'd like to file it under 'weird, won't
happen again.'"

**What it's hiding:** "non-recurring" items have a remarkable habit of recurring.
"Timing" is the universal solvent of bad results. Real discipline shows up in the
cash-flow statement, not the adjective budget of the press release.

## The Read — the five parts that matter *(paid)*

1. **Business (Item 1).** What they actually do and how they make money — in plain
   terms. If you can't explain it after this section, that's information.
2. **Risk Factors (Item 1A).** Skim the boilerplate, hunt the *changes.*
3. **MD&A (Item 7).** Management's story about the numbers. Read it, then check it
   against the actual statements — the gap between narrative and numbers is the tell.
4. **Financial statements + footnotes.** The footnotes are where the real accounting
   lives: revenue recognition, debt maturities, stock comp, the "we're being sued"
   section. Boring on purpose. Read them anyway.
5. **Cash flow statement.** Earnings are an opinion; cash is closer to a fact. Start
   here when the story and the income statement disagree.

## What I'd Watch

Pull the latest 10-K of a company you own. Read *only* Item 1A and diff it against
last year's. Fifteen minutes, and you'll know more than the person who watched a
ten-minute video about the same stock.
