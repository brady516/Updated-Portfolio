# The 4% rule, honestly

*Monthly flagship — the long one.*

"Withdraw 4% a year and you won't run out." It's the most repeated number in
retirement, treated as a law of physics. It's not a law — it's the result of one
study, on one country, over one set of decades, with assumptions worth knowing before
you bet thirty years of groceries on it. Useful starting point, terrible autopilot.

## Signal vs. Noise — "just take 4%" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** 4% as a permanent, set-and-forget guarantee.

The original work (the "Trinity study") found that a 4% initial withdrawal, raised
with inflation, survived most historical 30-year windows for a US stock/bond
portfolio. "Most historical windows" is not "guaranteed," "30 years" is not "however
long you live," and "US, last century" is not "the future."

> **The Signal:** 4% is a *starting estimate,* not a promise. The honest plan is a
> flexible rule, not a fixed percentage marched off a cliff.

## The Number — what 4% assumes *(free)*

4% bakes in: a ~30-year horizon, a stock-heavy portfolio held through crashes,
inflation raises every year regardless of markets, and US historical returns. Change
any one — retire at 55, go bond-heavy, hit a bad first decade (Issue #24) — and the
"safe" rate moves. It's a model output, not a covenant.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our income strategy is designed to deliver a sustainable,
inflation-adjusted withdrawal stream throughout retirement."*

**Translation:** "We'll send you a check and hope the math works."

**What it's hiding:** "sustainable" is the word on trial. Sustainable *under what
return sequence, what horizon, what flexibility?* A real plan states its failure rate
and its plan B. "Sustainable income stream" with no probability attached is the rosy
line from Issue #22 wearing a retirement hat.

## The Read — using 4% like a grown-up *(paid)*

- **Treat it as a dial, not a switch.** Start near 4% (lower if you retire early or
  hold lots of bonds; you may afford more if flexible). Then *adjust to reality.*
- **Add guardrails.** The flexible versions (e.g., Guyton-Klinger) say: cut the raise
  or trim spending after bad years, give yourself a bump after great ones. Modest
  flexibility raises your safe rate *and* your odds — far better than rigid 4%-forever.
- **Mind the first five years.** A crash early in retirement is the killer (sequence
  risk, Issue #24). A cash buffer + willingness to spend less early is worth more than
  any precise percentage.
- **Separate floors from upside.** Cover non-negotiable expenses with reliable income
  (Social Security, pensions, maybe a simple annuity — Issue #29); let the portfolio
  fund the flexible rest. You defend the essentials and let the variable stuff vary.
- **Recompute, don't autopilot.** "Am I on track?" is a question you re-ask yearly with
  real balances, not a decision you make once at 65 and never revisit.

The 4% rule's real gift isn't the number — it's the proof that a sane withdrawal rate
*exists.* The danger is treating a useful average as a guarantee and removing your hands
from the wheel for three decades.

## What I'd Watch

If your retirement plan is "take 4% and don't think about it," stress one thing: *what
do I do if my first two years are down 30%?* If the answer is "keep withdrawing 4% of
the original number and hope," you don't have a withdrawal *strategy* — you have a
withdrawal *wish.* Add a guardrail and a buffer, and the wish becomes a plan.
