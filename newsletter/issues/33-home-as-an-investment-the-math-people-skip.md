# Home as an investment? The math people skip

"Renting is throwing money away. Your home is the best investment you'll ever make."
It's the most repeated financial advice in America, passed down like a family recipe,
and it's half-true at best. A home is a *place to live* that sometimes also builds
wealth — but the "investment" case relies on conveniently forgetting most of the costs.

## Signal vs. Noise — "renting is throwing money away" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** rent = wasted money; a mortgage payment = "paying yourself."

Rent buys you shelter and flexibility — not "wasted," any more than groceries are
wasted. And a mortgage payment is mostly *interest* for years (you build little
equity early), plus you're now also paying property tax, insurance, maintenance, and
transaction costs that rent never charged you. Some of "owning" is forced savings;
much of it is just the cost of the house wearing a noble name.

> **The Signal:** compare *total* cost of owning (all of it) to *total* cost of renting
> *plus investing the difference.* Run that honestly before declaring a winner.

## The Number — the costs that vanish from the story *(free)*

The "my house doubled!" story almost never subtracts: **property tax** (~1–2%/yr),
**maintenance** (~1%/yr of value, forever), **insurance**, **mortgage interest**
(often more than the principal for the first decade), and **~6–10% in transaction
costs** to buy and sell. Net those out and the "great investment" frequently roughly
*tracks inflation* — real, but not the windfall the dinner-table story implies.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week — Real Estate Edition *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Real estate is a proven inflation hedge and a path to building
generational wealth through leveraged appreciation and forced savings."*

**Translation:** "Houses go up, and the mortgage makes you save."

**What it's hiding:** "leveraged appreciation" cuts *both* ways — leverage magnifies
losses too (ask anyone who bought in 2006). "Forced savings" is real and genuinely
valuable for many people — but it's a *behavioral* benefit, not an investment-return
one, and the phrase smuggles the costs out the back door.

## The Read — when owning wins, and when it doesn't *(paid)*

Owning tends to win when:
- **You stay put long enough** (roughly 5–7+ years) to amortize the brutal
  buy/sell transaction costs.
- **The rent-vs-buy math in your specific market favors it** — price-to-rent ratios
  vary enormously by city; the same salary buys very different math in different places.
- **You value the non-financial goods** — stability, control, no landlord — which are
  real even when the *investment* case is a wash.
- **The forced-savings discipline genuinely helps you** save when you otherwise wouldn't.

Renting tends to win when:
- **You might move** in a few years (transaction costs eat you alive).
- **Price-to-rent is sky-high** in your market (renting cheaply and investing the large
  difference beats owning).
- **You'll actually invest the difference** — the whole "rent and invest" case
  collapses if the savings just get spent.

The honest framing: **a home is primarily consumption that *can* double as a
decent-but-unspectacular forced-savings vehicle.** It's often a fine decision and a
poor *investment thesis.* Buy it because you want to live there on terms that make
sense — not because "renting is throwing money away," which is a slogan, not a
calculation.

## What I'd Watch

Before "it's always better to own," run the real comparison for *your* situation: total
ownership cost (including maintenance, tax, interest, transaction costs) vs. rent +
investing the difference, over how long you'll actually stay. If you've never run it,
you're repeating a recipe, not doing the math.
