# Asset bubbles: the story vs. the math

Every bubble runs on the same fuel: a *story* so good it makes the *math* feel rude to
mention. Tulips, dot-coms, housing, crypto cycles, whatever's next — the narrative is
always "this time the old rules don't apply." They always do. You can't reliably time
the top, but you can absolutely tell when you've wandered onto the casino floor.

## Signal vs. Noise — "this time is different" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** the four most expensive words in finance — *"this time is different."*
> New paradigm, new economy, the metric that used to matter doesn't anymore.

Sometimes the technology *is* genuinely new (the internet was real). The bubble isn't
in the technology — it's in the **price.** A real revolution and an insane valuation
can coexist; plenty of real-internet companies still vaporized investors in 2000. The
story being true doesn't make the price sane.

> **The Signal:** judge the *price*, not the *story.* "Great company" and "great
> investment at this price" are different questions, and bubbles exist to blur them.

## The Number — when valuation stops being mentioned *(free)*

The clearest bubble tell isn't a specific P/E — it's when **valuation stops being part
of the conversation entirely.** When "what does it earn / what is it worth" gets you
called a dinosaur, and price is justified only by *other people buying* (greater-fool),
the math has left the building. That's not analysis; that's musical chairs with
commentary.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Legacy valuation frameworks fail to capture the exponential,
paradigm-shifting total addressable market of this generational opportunity."*

**Translation:** "It's expensive and I don't want to talk about it."

**What it's hiding:** "legacy valuation frameworks fail to capture" is the bubble's
signature move — declaring the *measuring stick* obsolete rather than admitting the
*price* is extreme. "Generational opportunity" and "exponential TAM" are crowd-noise
where a number should be. The minute someone retires the ruler, hold onto your wallet.

## The Read — staying sane in a mania *(paid)*

You don't need to call the top (nobody can). You need to not get *destroyed*:

- **Anchor to cash flows, even loosely.** What would this have to earn, for how long,
  to justify the price? (Reverse-DCF, Issue #4.) If the implied future requires a
  miracle, you're paying for a story.
- **Watch the tells, not the ticker:** valuation dismissed as irrelevant, leverage and
  margin everywhere, total strangers giving hot tips, "can't lose," parabolic charts,
  new "experts" minted weekly. None times the top; together they're a weather report.
- **Size for the drawdown, not the dream.** If a position can't fall 70% without
  changing your life, it's too big — manias give back most or all of the move (Issue
  #3 on sizing).
- **Separate participation from belief.** It's fine to own some of a real trend. It's
  not fine to bet the house because the story is exciting. A small, sized sleeve lets
  you participate without being the greater fool holding the chair when the music stops.
- **Beware "I'll get out in time."** Everyone plans to. The exits are narrow and the
  door is on fire precisely when you decide to use it (Issue #16, liquidity).

The discipline isn't predicting the pop. It's refusing to confuse *a great story* with
*a sane price* — and sizing so the difference can't ruin you.

## What I'd Watch

Pick the hottest thing in your feed right now and ask one question out loud: *what does
the price assume has to happen?* If you can't answer — if the only case is "it keeps
going up because it's going up" — you're not looking at an investment. You're looking at
a chair, and counting on the music.
