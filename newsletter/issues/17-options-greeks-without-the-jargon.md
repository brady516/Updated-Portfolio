# Options Greeks without the jargon

The Greeks sound like a secret priesthood — delta, gamma, theta, vega — and the
priesthood likes it that way, because mystique sells courses. They're not mystical.
They're just four answers to four plain questions about what moves an option's price.
Learn the questions and the jargon stops working on you.

## Signal vs. Noise — "you don't need the Greeks to trade options" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** either "the Greeks are too complex for retail" *or* "ignore them,
> just buy calls." Both are wrong, in opposite directions.

You don't need the math PhD. You absolutely need the intuition — because if you don't
know what's moving your option's price, you're not trading, you're donating with
extra steps.

> **The Signal:** four questions, plain English. That's the whole starter kit.

## The Number — theta never sleeps *(free)*

An option is a melting ice cube. **Theta** is the melt rate — the value it loses every
single day just from time passing, even if the stock does nothing. Buy options and
time is your enemy; sell them and it's your paycheck. That one fact reorganizes how
most beginners think about "just buying calls."

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our options overlay harvests volatility risk premium while
dynamically managing the Greek exposures across the surface."*

**Translation:** "We sell options and adjust them."

**What it's hiding:** "harvests volatility risk premium" is a fancy way to say "sells
insurance and collects the premium" — a real strategy with a real tail risk it
doesn't mention. "Dynamically managing across the surface" means "we trade a lot,"
which means costs you're paying.

## The Read — the four questions *(paid)*

1. **Delta — "how much does my option move when the stock moves $1?"** A 0.50 delta
   ≈ moves ~$0.50 per $1 of stock. It's also a rough read on the odds of finishing
   in-the-money. Your directional exposure, in one number.
2. **Gamma — "how fast does that sensitivity itself change?"** High gamma = delta
   shifts quickly = the position gets twitchy near the strike and near expiry. It's
   the acceleration behind delta's speed.
3. **Theta — "what does waiting cost me per day?"** The melt. Negative when you own
   options, positive when you've sold them.
4. **Vega — "what happens if fear (implied volatility) rises or falls?"** Buy options
   when IV is low and spikes, you win on vega; buy when IV is sky-high and it
   collapses, you can be right on direction and *still lose.* This is how earnings
   plays burn people.

Put together: when you buy a call you're long delta (direction), long vega (fear),
and short theta (time's against you) — three bets in a trench coat. Most "it went up
but my call lost money" stories are vega and theta quietly doing their jobs.

## What I'd Watch

Before any options trade, say the position out loud in the four answers: *my
direction bet, my speed, my daily cost, my volatility bet.* If you can't, you don't
know what you own — and the person who sold it to you is counting on that.
