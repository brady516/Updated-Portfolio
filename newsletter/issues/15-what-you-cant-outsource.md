# What you can't outsource

I spent years quietly outsourcing my own wealth — to advisors, to funds, to whatever
tool promised to think so I didn't have to. Then I built a bot that encoded my own
trading behavior, watched it find an edge, and finally understood what I'd been
getting wrong the whole time. My problem was never a *skill* problem. It was an
*outsourcing* problem. I'd handed away the one part I should have kept.

## Signal vs. Noise — "our AI finds the alpha" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "Our AI-driven platform autonomously discovers alpha across the
> opportunity set." The machine finds the edge; you just fund it.

Here's the tell nobody says out loud: **anything a machine can find in public data,
every machine finds.** The moment an edge is discoverable by pattern-matching, it's
crowded and arbitraged before you can put it on. An edge a computer can hand you
isn't an edge — it's a coin flip with extra GPUs.

> **The Signal:** AI is a phenomenal *tester*. It is not a *finder*. It can generate
> candidates and grind them against history all night — but it can't tell you which
> one is real, why it should persist, or whether to bet against the consensus. That
> judgment is still yours.

## The Number — the shelf life of an obvious edge *(free)*

An edge that every model can see has a half-life measured in *months*, not years —
sometimes weeks. The durable ones aren't hiding in the data yet; they're sitting in
a thesis about *why* the world is mispricing something. You don't query that. You
*reason* it.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our proprietary AI autonomously discovers and harvests
persistent, uncorrelated alpha across a vast opportunity set."*

**Translation:** "Our computer found a backtest."

**What it's hiding:** "discovers alpha" quietly swaps *pattern-mining* (easy,
crowded, fragile) for *edge* (rare, reasoned, durable) and hopes you don't notice
the difference. "Persistent" is the word doing the most lying — the more
autonomously a machine found it from public data, the *less* persistent it is,
because everyone else's machine found it too.

## The Read — the outsource map *(paid)*

The fix isn't "do everything yourself." It's knowing exactly what to hand off and
what to guard with your life:

**Outsource to the machine — it's better than you:**
- Execution, monitoring, scheduling.
- Backtesting and validation (let it try to *kill* your idea — that's its best use).
- Scale, speed, tirelessness.

**Never outsource — this is the whole game:**
- **The finding.** The hypothesis, the "why," the place to look that nobody's pointing at.
- **The judgment.** Which candidate is real vs. a backtest mirage.
- **The conviction.** Holding a view the data doesn't confirm *yet.*

Outsource the second list and you haven't bought an edge — you've *rented someone
else's*, crowded and marked up. That's the entire managed-money industry: pay a fee
to hand away the only part with a durable edge. (See also: the manager gets paid first.)

My bot is the clean version of the division: I didn't ask it to find the edge. I
encoded an edge I'd *found* — in my own behavior, my own scars — and used the machine
to test and run it. Human finds. Machine tests. That's the frontier, and it's not
moving.

One unfair advantage in all this, and it's generational: if you came up analog and
went pro digital, you can *think from first principles* **and** *wield the machine.*
The fully-digital crowd over-trusts the model; the pre-digital crowd under-uses it.
The edge is in the narrow band that does both — find it like a human, test it like a
machine.

## What I'd Watch

Next time something promises that AI will *find* you returns, ask one question: *who
else's AI is looking at the same data?* If the answer is "everyone's," you've found
the noise. Use the machine to test what *you* found. The finding was always the job.
