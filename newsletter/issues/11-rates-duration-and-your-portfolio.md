# Rates, duration, and your portfolio

Interest rates are the gravity of finance — they quietly pull on the price of
everything, from your "safe" bond fund to your most speculative growth stock. Most
people who say rates don't affect them simply haven't noticed the mechanism. Here's
the mechanism.

## Signal vs. Noise — "rates don't matter if you're long-term" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "I'm long-term, so the rate cycle is noise."

Being long-term is a genuine edge. It does not make you immune to math. Rising rates
raise the discount applied to *all* future cash flows — which is to say, to the
entire basis on which your assets are priced. "Long-term" affects how you *respond*;
it doesn't repeal the gravity.

> **The Signal:** rates reprice everything through the discount rate. Know your
> exposure even if you never plan to trade it.

## The Number — duration *(free)*

Duration tells you roughly how much a bond (or bond fund) moves when rates move. A
duration of **7** means about a **7% price drop for a 1% rise** in rates. The
"boring safe bond fund" people held in 2022 fell hard for exactly this reason —
duration they never knew they owned.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We are positioned for a higher-for-longer regime while
remaining nimble to pivot as the data evolves and the Fed's reaction function
becomes clearer."*

**Translation:** "We don't know what rates will do, so we're hedging our words."

**What it's hiding:** "nimble to pivot" and "data-dependent" are how the pros say
"we'll change our minds and call it discipline." Nobody knows the rate path —
including the Fed. The honest version is shorter and admits it.

## The Read — where rates actually hit you *(paid)*

- **Bonds (directly).** Price moves inverse to rates, scaled by duration. Long bonds
  are a rate bet whether or not you meant to make one.
- **Long-duration equities.** Growth names whose value sits in far-future cash flows
  are the equity version of long bonds — they get repriced hardest when rates rise.
- **Leverage and refinancing.** Companies (and people) rolling debt at higher rates
  see real cash flow shrink. Check the maturity wall, not just the balance.
- **The hurdle rate for everything.** When "risk-free" T-bills pay a real yield,
  every risky asset has to clear a higher bar to be worth it. That bar moves prices.

You don't need to forecast rates — nobody can, reliably. You need to know your
exposure *before* the move, so a rate surprise is an inconvenience, not a margin call.

## What I'd Watch

Find the duration of your bond holdings and ask which of your stocks are "long
duration" (value mostly in the distant future). That's your rate exposure. Knowing
it beats being surprised by it every single time.
