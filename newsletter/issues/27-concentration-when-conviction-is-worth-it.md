# Concentration: when conviction is actually worth it

Issue #9 made the case that diversification isn't a *count* of stocks — it's owning
things that don't all fall together. This is the other half of the coin, and it's the
uncomfortable one: **diversification is the right default, and concentration is how
real fortunes are actually built.** Both are true. The skill is knowing which game
you're qualified to play, and sizing for the answer.

## Signal vs. Noise — "diversify, always, full stop" *(free)*

> **The Noise (one extreme):** "concentration is gambling; own everything." **The Noise
> (other extreme):** "diversification is for people with no conviction — go all in."

Both bumper stickers are wrong. Indexing is correct for the money you can't afford to
get wrong. Concentration is how the genuinely-informed turn an *edge* into *wealth* —
but only when the edge is real, and only sized so a mistake isn't terminal.

> **The Signal:** diversification protects you from what you don't know; concentration
> pays you for what you *do* — *if* you actually know it. The honesty is in admitting
> which is which.

## The Number — get-rich vs. stay-rich *(free)*

Concentration builds wealth; diversification keeps it. Nearly every great fortune was
*made* concentrated (a founder's stake, a few huge convictions) and *kept* diversified.
The error is using the wrong one for the wrong phase — diversifying away an edge you
genuinely have, or staying concentrated in the one position that already made you, right
up until it unmakes you.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We run a high-conviction, best-ideas portfolio to maximize
idiosyncratic alpha."*

**Translation:** "We made a few big bets."

**What it's hiding:** "high-conviction best-ideas" is concentration with a halo. It can
be brilliant or reckless and the phrase can't tell you which — only the *edge* and the
*sizing* can. Ask: conviction based on *what,* and sized so being wrong costs *what?*
Without those two answers, "high-conviction" is just "undiversified" in a nicer suit.

## The Read — earning the right to concentrate *(paid)*

Concentration is justified only when you can answer *yes* to all of these:

- **Do you have a real edge here?** A genuine informational, analytical, or temperamental
  advantage — you understand this deeply, or you'll hold through pain others won't. "I
  have a good feeling" is not an edge (Issue #15: the finding is the skill — but it has
  to be *real*).
- **Is the downside survivable at this size?** Concentrate with money that, if it went to
  zero, would hurt but not end you. The graveyard is full of people who were *right* and
  *too big* (Issue #3).
- **Can you tell conviction from attachment?** Conviction is "the thesis still holds."
  Attachment is "I've owned it forever / it already made me money." Re-underwrite the
  position as if you held cash today: would you still buy it here?
- **Will you trim a winner that's become your whole portfolio?** The stock that 10x'd is
  now an undiversified bet you didn't consciously choose. Letting winners run is fine
  *inside the thesis*; letting one become 60% of your net worth by accident is just drift
  (Issue #21).

The mature structure most people land on: a **diversified core** you can't afford to get
wrong, plus a **sized, deliberate concentrated sleeve** for the few convictions where you
genuinely have an edge — and the discipline to keep the sleeve from quietly eating the
core. Concentrate to *get* wealthy; diversify to *stay* wealthy; never confuse which
phase you're in.

## What I'd Watch

Look at your largest position and answer honestly: is it big because you *chose* it at
this size today on a live thesis — or because it ran and you never trimmed? The first is
conviction. The second is drift wearing conviction's clothes, and the market eventually
charges you to tell them apart.
