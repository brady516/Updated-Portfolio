# Tax drag: the other silent fee

We spent a whole issue on the expense ratio — the fee the manager takes first. Here's
its quieter twin: **taxes.** Not the rate you grumble about in April, but the steady
*drag* of paying tax on gains and income you didn't need to realize yet. Like fees, it
sounds small and compounds into a number that should make you wince.

## Signal vs. Noise — "my fund returned 12%" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** judging by the headline return, pre-tax, in a taxable account.

That 12% is the brochure number. The number that buys groceries is *after-tax,
after-fee.* A high-turnover fund that kicks off short-term gains can hand you a great
pre-tax return and a surprisingly mediocre keep-rate, because Uncle Sam is also a
limited partner you didn't interview.

> **The Signal:** track what you *keep*, not what you *earn.* After-tax, after-fee, or
> you're scoring the wrong game.

## The Number — turnover = tax *(free)*

A fund with 100% annual turnover is realizing gains constantly — and short-term gains
are taxed as ordinary income, the highest rate there is. A low-turnover index fund can
defer gains for *decades.* Same market exposure, wildly different tax bill, just from
how often the thing trades.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our actively managed strategy seeks to deliver
tax-aware, after-tax alpha through thoughtful portfolio construction."*

**Translation:** "We trade a lot, but we'll try not to make the tax bill *too* bad."

**What it's hiding:** "tax-aware" is a tell that taxes are a *problem the strategy
creates* — a genuinely tax-efficient vehicle (a broad index fund) rarely needs to
advertise "tax-aware," because there's not much tax to be aware of.

## The Read — keeping more of what you make *(paid)*

- **Asset location ≠ asset allocation.** *What* you own matters; *which account* you
  own it in matters almost as much. Tax-inefficient stuff (bonds, REITs, high-turnover
  funds) belongs in tax-sheltered accounts (IRA/401k); tax-efficient stuff (index
  funds, long-held stocks) can sit in taxable. Same holdings, better keep-rate, zero
  extra risk.
- **Defer, defer, defer.** An unrealized gain is an interest-free loan from the
  government that compounds for you. Every unnecessary sale calls the loan.
- **Harvest losses on purpose.** Losers in a taxable account have a silver lining —
  realize the loss to offset gains, mind the wash-sale rule, keep your exposure.
- **Turnover is a tax decision, not just a strategy one.** Every trade in a taxable
  account is a potential taxable event. "Active" has a tax cost the backtest never shows.

None of this is exotic, and *that's the point* — like fees, tax drag is boring,
ignorable, and enormous over time. The boring optimizations are where the real money
quietly is.

## What I'd Watch

Pull last year's 1099 and find what your taxable funds *distributed* — the gains you
paid tax on whether you sold or not. If a fund is generating short-term gains you
didn't ask for, it's charging you a second fee, payable to the IRS. Worth knowing
before you owe it.
