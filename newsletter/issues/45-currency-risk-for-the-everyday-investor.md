# Currency risk for the everyday investor

When you buy international stocks or funds, you're making two bets, not one: a bet on
the *companies* and a bet on the *currency* they trade in. Most people only know they
made the first. The second — the dollar rising or falling against the euro, yen, or
pound — quietly adds or subtracts from your returns, and nobody mentioned it at checkout.

## Signal vs. Noise — "I own international for diversification" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "International funds diversify me" — full stop, no mention of FX.

True, and incomplete. Owning a European fund means owning European *companies* **and**
the *euro.* If the euro falls vs. the dollar, your returns shrink in dollar terms even
if those companies did fine. You diversified your *stocks* and added a *currency*
exposure you may not have chosen on purpose.

> **The Signal:** an unhedged foreign holding is a *two-part bet* — the asset and the
> currency. Know you're making both, and decide whether you want the second.

## The Number — the FX drag (or boost) *(free)*

Currency swings are large: major pairs can move **10–15%+ in a year.** That can *erase*
a good year of foreign stock gains — or *amplify* them. Over the long run currency moves
tend to wash out somewhat, but over any *given* few years they're big enough to be the
difference between a winning and losing international position.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our global strategy provides diversified international exposure
with dynamic, active currency overlay management to optimize risk-adjusted returns."*

**Translation:** "We bet on foreign stocks and also trade currencies, for a fee."

**What it's hiding:** "active currency overlay" means *someone is also speculating on
FX* with your money — a notoriously hard game (Issue #15: an edge a machine and a
thousand banks can see isn't an edge). "Dynamic" usually means "more trading, more
cost." Often you'd rather have a *simple, cheap, currency-hedged* fund than a pricey
"overlay."

## The Read — hedged, unhedged, or don't-overthink-it *(paid)*

- **Equities: unhedged is usually fine — for the long term.** Over decades, currency
  effects on foreign *stocks* tend to roughly wash, and hedging costs money. Most
  long-horizon investors can hold unhedged international equity and not lose sleep. Just
  *know* the currency exposure is there, especially over shorter windows.
- **Bonds: hedging matters more.** Foreign *bond* returns are smaller, so currency
  swings can swamp them. A currency move can dwarf the entire yield. For international
  bonds, a **currency-hedged** fund usually makes more sense than unhedged.
- **Hedged vs. unhedged funds — know which you own.** "Hedged" funds neutralize the
  currency (you get the foreign *asset* return, minus a small hedging cost). "Unhedged"
  gives you asset + currency. Neither is wrong; they're *different bets,* and the
  ticker rarely shouts which.
- **Don't pay up for currency *speculation*.** "Active currency overlay" is a fee to
  let someone gamble on FX — a near-random game most managers don't win net of cost.
- **Mind your *home-country* lens.** You spend in dollars, so dollar strength/weakness
  is what ultimately matters to your purchasing power — not the local-currency return
  the foreign market reports.

For most everyday investors the takeaway is calm: own some international equity
*unhedged* and don't fret the FX noise; for international *bonds,* lean *hedged;* and
ignore anyone selling "currency overlay" as alpha. The mistake isn't having currency
exposure — it's having it *without knowing,* and then being baffled when a good foreign
year shows up red in your dollar account.

## What I'd Watch

Check whether your international funds are **hedged or unhedged** (the fund page says).
Then you'll know whether a foreign holding that "should" be up but isn't is a *company*
story or a *currency* story — and whether you chose that second bet or it chose you.
