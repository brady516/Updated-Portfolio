# Share buybacks: returning capital or propping the chart?

Companies hand back tens of billions a year buying their own stock, and the takes split
into two religions: "buybacks are shareholder-friendly capital return" and "buybacks
are financial engineering that enriches executives." Like most religious wars, both
sides are sometimes right. The trick is telling a smart buyback from a dumb one.

## Signal vs. Noise — "buybacks always boost shareholder value" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "A buyback returns cash to shareholders and lifts EPS — always good."

A buyback only *creates* value if the stock is bought **below** its intrinsic value —
same rule as you buying any stock. Companies are notorious for buying *high* (flush with
cash at the top) and stopping *low* (scared in a crash) — the exact opposite of the job.
"Boosts EPS" is also true *mechanically* (fewer shares) without the business improving
one bit.

> **The Signal:** a buyback is the company *buying its own stock.* Judge it the way
> you'd judge any purchase — *at what price, with whose money, instead of what?*

## The Number — EPS without earnings *(free)*

Buy back enough shares and **EPS rises even if total profit is flat or falling** —
you're dividing the same (or smaller) pie among fewer slices. So "record EPS, up 10%!"
can mean the business grew, *or* just that share count shrank. Always check earnings
growth *and* share-count change before you credit management for "growth."

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"We remain committed to returning capital to shareholders and
enhancing per-share value through our disciplined repurchase program."*

**Translation:** "We're buying our stock, and we'd like credit for it regardless of
price."

**What it's hiding:** "disciplined repurchase program" rarely specifies the *discipline*
— i.e., a price below which they buy and above which they stop. Without that, "disciplined"
is decoration. And "enhancing per-share value" can just mean *shrinking the share count,*
which isn't the same as building the business.

## The Read — smart buyback vs. dumb buyback *(paid)*

**A good buyback:**
- Happens when the stock is **below** the company's intrinsic value (Issue #4) — buying
  a dollar for eighty cents genuinely helps remaining owners.
- Uses **genuine excess cash,** after the business is funded (capex, R&D, debt paydown).
- Is **opportunistic,** not on a fixed schedule that buys most at the top.

**A red-flag buyback:**
- **Buying high** because cash is sloshing around at the cycle peak (then halting in the
  crash, locking in the bad timing).
- **Debt-funded** buybacks that lever up the balance sheet to goose EPS — fragile when
  the cycle turns (Issue #16).
- **Offsetting dilution** from heavy executive stock comp — the buyback quietly funds
  management's pay while *net* share count barely drops. Watch *net* buybacks, not gross.
- **Propping a metric** tied to executive bonuses (EPS targets) rather than serving
  owners.

The honest verdict: buybacks aren't inherently good or evil — they're a *capital
allocation decision,* and management's track record at it tells you a lot about the
whole company. A team that buys its own stock cheap and stops when it's dear is showing
you discipline. A team that buys high, on debt, to hit a bonus target is showing you
something else — and both are saying it in the same press release.

## What I'd Watch

Next "record share buyback" headline, check two numbers: did **earnings** actually grow,
or just **EPS** (via fewer shares)? And is the **net** share count really falling, or is
the buyback just mopping up executive stock grants? The gap between the headline and
those two numbers is the whole story.
