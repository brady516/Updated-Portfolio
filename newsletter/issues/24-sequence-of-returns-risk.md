# Sequence-of-returns risk: why the order ruins you

*Monthly flagship — the long one.*

Here's a fact that breaks most people's intuition: two retirees can earn the *exact
same average return* over the *exact same years* and one dies wealthy while the other
runs out of money — purely because of the **order** the returns arrived in. While
you're saving, sequence barely matters. The day you start *withdrawing*, it becomes
the most important risk in your financial life, and almost nobody plans for it.

## Signal vs. Noise — "it averages out" *(free)*

> **The Noise:** "Markets average ~7%, so over time it all evens out."

Averaging-out is true for a *lump sum left alone.* It is dangerously false when you're
*adding* money (dollar-cost averaging helps you) or *withdrawing* money (sequence hurts
you). When you sell shares to live on, a crash early in retirement permanently shrinks
the base that's supposed to recover. There's no "averaging out" the shares you already
spent at the bottom.

> **The Signal:** in retirement, *when* the bad years hit matters as much as *whether*
> they do. The order is the risk.

## The Number — same average, opposite endings *(free)*

Take two retirees, same 6% average over 25 years, same withdrawals. Give one a
brutal -20%/-15% in years 1–2; give the other those same bad years at the *end.*
The early-loss retiree can run out a decade sooner — same returns, same math, opposite
life. Selling shares into an early crash is a wound that compounds; you never get those
shares back to ride the recovery.

## Wall Street Word Salad of the Week *(paid)*

**This week's salad:** *"Our glide-path strategy dynamically optimizes the
risk/return profile to align with the client's evolving time horizon."*

**Translation:** "We hold fewer stocks as you age."

**What it's hiding:** the fancy phrasing buries a genuinely important idea (de-risk
around the retirement date) under enough jargon that you can't tell whether they're
*actually* managing sequence risk or just charging you to own a target-date fund you
could buy yourself. Ask the plain question: "what specifically protects me from a
crash in my first five years?"

## The Read — defusing the bomb *(paid)*

The danger zone is the **~5 years on either side of your retirement date.** A crash
there does outsized, permanent damage. Defenses, roughly in order of leverage:

- **A cash/bond buffer ("bucket").** Hold 1–3 years of spending in cash and short
  bonds so a bad early year means selling *those*, not your stocks at the bottom. You
  give the equities time to recover instead of harvesting them dead.
- **Flexible withdrawals.** Spend less in down years. Even modest flexibility
  ("skip the inflation raise after a down year," guardrails) dramatically improves
  survival in the simulations. Rigid 4%-forever is the fragile version.
- **De-risk into the zone, re-risk after.** Lower equity exposure approaching the
  date when sequence risk peaks; it can drift back up later (a "rising equity glide
  path") since the danger window passes.
- **Keep some growth.** Over-correcting to all-bonds creates the *opposite* failure —
  inflation slowly starves a 30-year retirement. The fix for sequence risk isn't
  fleeing stocks; it's not being a *forced seller* of them at the wrong moment.

The through-line: **don't be a forced seller into a crash.** Every defense above is a
way to buy time so the recovery can do its job. (This is also why a Monte Carlo that
models *withdrawal order* — Issue #22 — beats any single-line projection: the rosy line
literally can't see this risk.)

## What I'd Watch

If you're within ~10 years of retirement, ask one question of your plan: *if the market
fell 30% the year I retire, what would I have to sell, and would I recover?* If you
don't have a buffer or a flex-spending rule, you don't have a sequence plan — you have
a hope that the order is kind. It often isn't.
